Understanding B&Q Kitchen Unit Sizes and Ranges

B&Q, through its Bathrooms and Kitchens arm, offers an extensive range of flat-pack and rigid kitchen units across multiple style collections. Before you start planning, it helps to understand the standard sizes you'll be working with.

Standard Base Unit Dimensions

Base units from B&Q typically come in the following widths:

  • 300mm — narrow units, often used for pull-out storage or end fillers
  • 400mm — useful for smaller gaps and compact kitchens
  • 500mm — a popular mid-size option
  • 600mm — the most common width, ideal for sinks and hobs
  • 800mm and 1000mm — wider units for corner solutions and large pan drawers

Standard base unit height is 720mm (excluding worktop), and depth is typically 560mm or 600mm depending on the range. Wall units generally sit at 720mm or 900mm high and are shallower at around 300mm deep.

Tall and Tower Units

Larder units, oven housing towers, and full-height units typically stand at 1970mm or 2100mm high. These become important anchor points when planning your layout, as they often sit at the ends of a run or frame an appliance like an integrated oven.

Worktop Considerations

B&Q worktops are commonly sold in 3-metre lengths and 38mm or 40mm thickness. You'll need to account for these dimensions when planning around appliances, particularly if you're planning a breakfast bar or island extension.

How to Plan Your Kitchen Layout: A Step-by-Step Approach

Rushing into ordering B&Q kitchen units without a proper plan is a recipe for frustration. Follow these steps to get a layout that truly works for your space.

  1. Measure your room accurately — Use a tape measure and record the width and length of every wall. Note the position of windows, doors (and which way they open), radiators, and any structural features like chimney breasts or load-bearing walls.
  2. Mark fixed services — Note where your existing gas supply, soil pipe, boiler flue, and electrical sockets are. Moving these adds significant cost, so designing around them where possible saves money.
  3. Decide on your layout type — Galley, L-shape, U-shape, single-wall, and island layouts all have pros and cons depending on your room size and how you cook.
  4. Apply the kitchen work triangle — The classic design principle positions your hob, sink, and fridge in a triangle to minimise unnecessary movement while cooking.
  5. Draw it out to scale — This is where a digital planner is invaluable. Use our free kitchen planner tool to draw your walls to scale using the snap-to-grid system (snapping to 10cm increments) and then add units and appliances to see exactly what fits.
  6. Experiment with alternatives — Try multiple configurations before settling on one. It costs nothing to move a virtual unit, but moving a real one after installation is expensive and disruptive.
  7. Check clearances — Ensure there's at least 1200mm between facing units or appliances so two people can work in the kitchen comfortably. Check that dishwasher and oven doors can open fully.

Popular Kitchen Layout Styles and How They Work With B&Q Units

Galley Kitchen

Two parallel runs of units facing each other. Works brilliantly in narrow rooms and can be highly efficient if the work triangle is well considered. B&Q's slimline units at 560mm depth can help maximise the central aisle width in tighter spaces.

L-Shaped Kitchen

Units along two adjacent walls, often with a corner unit (carousel or dead corner storage solution). This layout suits medium-sized rooms and creates a natural separation between cooking and dining areas. B&Q offers dedicated corner base units designed for exactly this configuration.

U-Shaped Kitchen

Units on three walls for maximum storage and worktop space. Ideal for larger kitchens. Requires careful planning of the two corner junctions, and our floor planner makes it easy to visualise where each unit sits.

Open Plan With an Island

Increasingly popular in modern homes, this layout pairs wall units and base units on one or two sides with a freestanding or built-in island. B&Q offers island base units and breakfast bar supports that can be incorporated into your plan. Common Mistakes to Avoid When Ordering B&Q Kitchen Units

Even experienced renovators can fall into these traps. Being aware of them — and planning carefully in advance — can save you significant time and money.

  • Forgetting fillers and end panels — Kitchens rarely divide perfectly into standard unit widths. Filler strips and decorative end panels are essential finishing pieces that need to be included in your order.
  • Not accounting for appliance depths — Some integrated appliances, particularly American-style fridge freezers or range cookers, are deeper than standard units. This can create an awkward step in your worktop line.
  • Overlooking the ceiling height — If you want wall units to go all the way to the ceiling, check whether you need an additional pelmet or top unit, and measure your ceiling height carefully.
  • Ignoring ventilation requirements — A cooker hood needs to be positioned at the correct height above your hob and may require ducting through an external wall or into a ceiling void. Plan this before ordering units around that area.
  • Underestimating delivery lead times — Some B&Q kitchen ranges are available in store, but bespoke colours or sizes may have longer lead times. Always confirm availability before booking your kitchen fitter.

Tips for Working With a Kitchen Fitter or Contractor

If you're planning to hire a professional to install your B&Q kitchen units, preparation is everything. A good kitchen fitter will appreciate a client who arrives with a clear plan rather than a vague idea.

  • Bring your exported floor plan from our kitchen planner to your first meeting
  • Have your room measurements double-checked by the fitter before ordering anything
  • Discuss any tricky spots — chimney breasts, awkward ceiling angles, or pipework locations — in advance
  • Ask specifically whether the fitter is experienced with flat-pack fitting, as techniques differ from rigid unit installation
  • Confirm who is responsible for ordering worktops, and whether they'll be cut on site or pre-cut
