The Complete Guide to Kitchen Layouts: Which Type Works Best for Your Home?

March 2026 4 min read Try Free Room Planner free

Before you choose a colour, a worktop material, or an appliance brand, there is one decision that shapes everything else: the layout. Get the layout wrong and no amount of expensive finishes will make the kitchen work. Get it right and the room almost runs itself.

Here is a breakdown of the four most common kitchen layouts, when each one works, when it doesn't, and how to decide which one suits your room.

Why layout comes before everything else

Style is reversible. You can repaint cabinets, swap handles, change a splashback. Layout is not. Once the plumbing is in, the electrics are chased, and the units are fitted, moving the sink or the hob means starting again. That is why the first conversation with any kitchen designer should be about the shape of the room and how you move through it — not about what colour you want the doors.

The galley kitchen

A galley kitchen runs two parallel rows of units along opposite walls with a corridor between them. It is the most efficient layout for cooking because everything is within arm's reach and the workflow is linear: fridge at one end, preparation in the middle, cooking at the other.

It works best in narrow rooms — typically 2.4 metres wide or more between unit fronts. Below that, two people cannot pass each other comfortably. If your kitchen is a thoroughfare to the garden or utility room, a galley can feel cramped because people walk through the workspace. In a closed room with one entrance, it is hard to beat.

The L-shaped kitchen

Units run along two adjacent walls, forming an L. This is the most versatile layout and the one that works in the widest range of room shapes. It creates a natural work triangle between the hob, sink, and fridge without forcing you into a corridor.

An L-shape works particularly well in kitchen-diners, where the open side of the L faces the dining area. The key mistake people make is putting the sink and the hob on the same leg — this creates a long linear workflow that defeats the purpose of the L. Split them across the two legs and keep the fridge at the end of one leg where it does not interrupt the worktop run.

The U-shaped kitchen

Units wrap around three walls, giving you the most storage and worktop space of any layout. The work triangle is tight, everything is close, and there is usually enough room for two people to work without colliding.

The catch is space. You need at least 3 metres between the two parallel walls to fit a U-shape with adequate clearances. Below that, the room feels boxed in and cabinet doors clash when opened simultaneously. If you have the width, a U-shape is exceptionally functional. If you are forcing it into a room that is too narrow, an L-shape will serve you better.

The island kitchen

An island adds a freestanding unit to the centre of the kitchen, providing extra worktop, storage, and often seating. It can be paired with an L-shaped or U-shaped perimeter layout.

The space requirement is the thing people most often underestimate. You need a minimum of one metre of clearance on all four sides of the island — and 1.2 metres on any side where someone might be cooking while another person walks past. In practice, this means the room needs to be at least 4 metres wide before an island becomes viable. Draw it to scale before you fall in love with the idea. Many kitchens that look large enough by eye turn out to be 30 centimetres too narrow when measured.

How to work out which layout suits your room

Start with two things: the room dimensions and the positions of the door and window. The door dictates traffic flow — where people enter and exit. The window dictates where natural light falls and often where the sink ends up. These two fixed points narrow the layout options considerably.

Next, consider how the kitchen connects to other spaces. If it opens into a dining room, an L-shape or island layout keeps the cook facing the room. If it is a standalone room with one door, a galley or U-shape maximises every wall. If it is part of an open-plan space, the island often becomes the boundary between cooking and living.

Draw the layout to scale before any supplier visit

Kitchen designers will draw your layout for you — but they will draw it around the products they sell. If you arrive with your own scaled floor plan showing the layout you want, the conversation starts in a different place. You are no longer asking what is possible. You are showing what you have already worked out and asking them to refine it.

This does not need to be professional. It needs to be accurate. Measure your room, draw the walls to scale, place the units where you think they should go, and check the clearances. Ten minutes of planning prevents months of regret.
