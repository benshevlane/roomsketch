Understanding B&Q Kitchen Unit Sizes

One of the key advantages of buying B&Q kitchen units is that they follow standard UK kitchen sizing conventions, which makes planning much more straightforward. Here's a quick overview of what to expect:

Base Units

  • Height: Typically 720mm (designed to sit under a standard worktop height of 900mm once legs and worktop are added)
  • Depth: 570mm (standard, to accommodate a 600mm deep worktop with a slight overhang)
  • Width: Available in 150mm, 300mm, 400mm, 500mm, 600mm, and 1000mm widths

Wall Units

  • Height: 600mm or 900mm tall options available
  • Depth: 300mm standard
  • Width: Typically 300mm, 400mm, 500mm, and 600mm

Tall Units

  • Height: 1970mm or 2150mm, designed for integrated appliances or pantry storage
  • Depth: 570mm or 600mm depending on range
  • Width: Typically 600mm

Understanding these dimensions is critical when you're laying out your kitchen. Even a 50mm miscalculation can mean a unit doesn't fit, an appliance won't slot in, or a door won't open properly. That's exactly why drawing your kitchen to scale — before you buy anything — is so important.

The Most Common Kitchen Layout Types

Before choosing which B&Q kitchen units to buy, you need to decide on your kitchen layout. The right layout will depend on the shape and size of your room, where your plumbing and electrics are located, and how you use the space. Here are the most popular options:

Galley Kitchen

Two parallel runs of units facing each other. Ideal for narrow rooms or open-plan spaces. Efficient for cooking but can feel tight if less than 1.2 metres wide between units.

L-Shaped Kitchen

Units run along two adjacent walls, forming an L-shape. Works well in square rooms and allows for a kitchen island or dining table in the remaining space.

U-Shaped Kitchen

Units on three walls, wrapping around the cook. Maximises storage and worktop space. Best suited to larger rooms — at least 3 metres wide to allow comfortable movement.

Single Wall Kitchen

All units along one wall. Common in flats and open-plan living spaces. Space-efficient but limits worktop and storage capacity.

Island Kitchen

Any of the above layouts with a freestanding island unit in the centre. Great for entertaining and adds extra worktop space, but needs a minimum room width of around 3.5 to 4 metres.

Tips for Planning a Kitchen That Works

Even with the right units and a good floor plan, kitchens can fall short if the fundamentals aren't right. Here are some tried-and-tested planning principles to keep in mind:

The Kitchen Work Triangle

The classic design principle states that your three main work points — the sink, the hob, and the fridge — should form a triangle with no single side shorter than 1.2 metres or longer than 2.7 metres. Keeping these three elements within easy reach of each other reduces unnecessary movement during cooking. When placing your appliances in the kitchen planner, check whether your layout respects this principle.

Allow for Adequate Clearance

In any kitchen, you need enough space to open doors and drawers without obstruction. Key clearance rules to follow include:

  • At least 1 metre of clear floor space in front of base units (ideally 1.2 metres)
  • At least 400mm between the hob and any overhead wall unit or extractor housing
  • Enough room to open the oven door fully without it hitting an island or opposite unit

Think About Services Early

Your sink position is largely dictated by where your existing plumbing runs. Moving plumbing is possible but costly. Similarly, the position of your gas supply or electrical sockets will influence where your hob and oven sit. Try to design your layout around your existing service positions where possible — it can save hundreds of pounds in plumbing and electrical work.

Maximise Corner Space

Corner units are notorious for being awkward and underused. B&Q offer corner base units and carousels that make use of these areas more effectively. When planning your layout, consider whether a corner carousel, pull-out corner unit, or an angled unit might suit your needs better than leaving the corner dead.

Getting the Most Out of B&Q Kitchen Units

B&Q offers several kitchen ranges at different price points, from the entry-level ranges through to more premium shaker and handleless styles. Here are a few things to consider when selecting units:

Carcass Quality

The carcass is the box structure of the unit — the part that actually carries the load. B&Q kitchen units use moisture-resistant board for most carcasses, which is important in a kitchen environment. Check the thickness (18mm is standard; anything thinner may be less robust) and the quality of the cam and dowel fixings.

Door Style and Finish

Door styles range from simple slab designs to detailed shaker profiles. Think about how the style will work with the rest of your home. If your house has traditional features, a classic shaker door in a neutral tone will blend well. For a modern home, a handleless gloss or matt design might be more appropriate.

Soft-Close Hinges and Drawer Runners

Check whether soft-close hinges and drawer runners are included or need to be purchased separately. These are small components but make a big difference to how the kitchen feels in daily use.

Mixing with Other Brands

Because B&Q kitchen units follow standard dimensions, it's usually possible to mix their carcasses with doors or worktops from other suppliers. Some homeowners buy B&Q carcasses and fit them with doors from specialist door suppliers for a more bespoke look at a lower cost. Just verify compatibility before committing.
