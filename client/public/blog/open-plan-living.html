Planning an Open-Plan Living Space: Kitchen, Dining, and Lounge in One Room

March 2026 4 min read Try Free Room Planner free

Knocking through to create an open-plan kitchen, dining, and living space is one of the most popular home renovations in the UK. It is also one of the easiest to get wrong. The mistake most people make is treating the result as one room. It is not. It is three zones that share a single space — and each zone needs to work on its own terms while coexisting with the other two.

The planning mistake: treating it as one room instead of three zones

When people plan an open-plan space, they tend to start with the biggest item — usually the sofa — and work outward. Everything else fills in around it. The result is a room where the kitchen is cramped, the dining table is jammed against a wall, and the sofa faces the hob.

Start differently. Divide the floor plan into three zones: kitchen, dining, and living. Give each zone its own footprint. Plan each one as if it were a separate room that happens to share walls with the others. Only after each zone works independently should you think about how they connect.

How to define zones without walls

In a walled house, rooms define themselves. In an open-plan space, you need to create boundaries using other cues:

  • Furniture positioning: The back of a sofa, the edge of a dining table, or a kitchen island can mark where one zone ends and another begins
  • Flooring changes: Tile in the kitchen, timber in the living area — a change of material signals a change of zone
  • Ceiling treatments: A dropped ceiling bulkhead over the kitchen area or pendant lights over the dining table define the zone from above
  • Rugs: A rug under the dining table or under the sofa group anchors each zone visually

None of these require building work. All of them should be decided at the layout stage, not as afterthoughts.

The kitchen triangle in an open-plan space

The work triangle — the path between the fridge, sink, and hob — is the foundation of any functional kitchen. In a closed kitchen, the triangle is contained. In an open-plan space, it risks being interrupted by through-traffic from the dining area or the back door.

Keep the triangle tight and away from the main walking routes. If the kitchen has an island, use the island as one point of the triangle (typically the hob or the sink). The key test: can someone walk from the back door to the sofa without crossing between the cook and the worktop? If not, the layout needs adjusting.

Traffic flow: the route from the back door to the sofa

In most open-plan spaces, people enter from the garden through the kitchen end and walk to the living area. This route — back door to sofa — dictates everything. If it cuts through the kitchen triangle, cooking becomes interrupted. If it passes too close to the dining table, seated diners get bumped.

Draw this route on your floor plan as a line. Then draw the route from the front door. And the route from the stairs. Every one of these paths needs to be at least 90 centimetres wide and should not pass through a zone — it should pass between them.

Where the dining table goes

The dining table is the hinge of the entire layout. It sits between the kitchen and the living area, functioning as the transition point. Place it too close to the kitchen and diners feel like they are sitting in a workspace. Place it too close to the sofa and the living area shrinks.

The table needs 90 centimetres of clearance on all sides for chairs to pull out. In practice, this means a table for six occupies roughly 2.5 by 3 metres of floor space including clearances. Mark this zone on your plan before placing anything else — it is the most spatially demanding item in the room.

Noise and sight lines

Open-plan living means the extractor fan runs while you watch television. It means washing-up noise carries to the sofa. It means every dirty pan is visible from the dining table. These are not design flaws — they are trade-offs that come with the format.

You can mitigate them. A quiet extractor fan matters more in an open-plan kitchen than in a closed one. A kitchen island with a raised back panel hides the worktop mess from the dining area. Positioning the television on the wall furthest from the kitchen reduces noise interference. But you cannot eliminate these issues entirely. Open-plan means open.

How to draw an open-plan space on a floor plan

Draw the outer walls of the entire space as one room. Then draw light boundary lines to divide it into three zones. Plan the kitchen zone first — it has the most fixed constraints (plumbing, gas, extraction). Plan the dining zone second — it needs the most clearance. Plan the living zone last — it is the most flexible.

Label each zone. Check the traffic routes. Verify that no walking path cuts through a zone. Then — and only then — start placing furniture.

Draw your open-plan space as three zones, not one room. It changes how the layout decisions make sense.
