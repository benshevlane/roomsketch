Understanding B&Q Kitchen Unit Sizes and Ranges

B&Q offers a wide range of kitchen units under their own brand and through their Cooke & Lewis range. Before you start planning, it helps to understand the standard sizing conventions used across their cabinets.

Base Units

Base units sit on the floor and form the worktop-bearing backbone of your kitchen. Standard dimensions are:

  • Height: Typically 720mm (before worktop and legs)
  • Depth: Usually 570mm or 600mm
  • Width: Ranges from 150mm to 1000mm, with common widths at 300mm, 400mm, 500mm, 600mm, and 800mm

Wall Units

Wall units are mounted above the worktop and provide additional storage without taking up floor space. Common dimensions include:

  • Height: 360mm, 576mm, 720mm, or 900mm
  • Depth: Around 300mm
  • Width: 300mm to 800mm, matching base unit widths

Tall Units

Tall units — sometimes called larder units or tower units — run floor to ceiling and are ideal for housing integrated appliances like ovens or for creating a dedicated pantry. Heights typically reach 1970mm to 2150mm.

Filler Panels and Accessories

Don't forget that B&Q also sells filler panels, plinths, cornices, and end panels that are essential for finishing off a fitted look. These often get overlooked in the planning stage and can throw off your measurements if not accounted for early.

How to Measure Your Kitchen for B&Q Units

Accurate measurements are absolutely critical when ordering kitchen units. Even a 10mm error can mean a unit doesn't fit or that you have an unsightly gap in your run of cabinets. Here's how to measure your kitchen correctly:

  1. Start with the overall room dimensions: Measure the length and width of your kitchen at floor level. Note any irregularities — walls are rarely perfectly square.
  2. Measure each wall individually: Measure from corner to corner along each wall, noting the position and size of windows and doors.
  3. Mark obstructions: Note the position of radiators, pipes, boilers, fuse boxes, and any structural columns or alcoves.
  4. Record window and door heights: Measure from the floor to the bottom of the window sill and from the floor to the top of the door frame — this affects where wall units can be positioned.
  5. Locate existing plumbing and electrics: Note where your water supply, waste pipe, and electrical sockets are positioned, as these will influence where your sink, dishwasher, and appliances sit.
  6. Double-check everything: Measure each wall at least twice, and measure at both floor and ceiling level — you may find they differ slightly in older properties.

Once you have all your measurements, you're ready to start planning your layout digitally.

Popular Kitchen Layouts to Consider

Not sure which layout suits your space? Here are the most common kitchen configurations and when each one works best:

Galley Kitchen

Two parallel runs of units facing each other. Ideal for narrow kitchens or open-plan spaces where the kitchen runs along one wall. Highly efficient for cooking as everything is within easy reach.

L-Shaped Kitchen

Units arranged along two adjacent walls, forming an L shape. One of the most popular layouts for medium-sized kitchens. Leaves plenty of floor space and works well with a dining table in the corner of the room.

U-Shaped Kitchen

Units on three walls, maximising storage and worktop space. Best suited to larger kitchens. Can feel enclosed in smaller rooms, so make sure you have at least 1.2 metres of clearance between opposing runs.

Island Kitchen

A central island unit adds extra worktop space, storage, and often a seating area. Works best in larger, open-plan kitchens. B&Q offer freestanding island units as well as modular base units that can be used to build a custom island.

Tips for Getting the Most from B&Q Kitchen Units

Once you've planned your layout, here are some practical tips to make the most of your B&Q kitchen units when ordering and installing:

  • Order sample doors: B&Q offer door samples so you can see the colour and finish in your own home under your own lighting before committing.
  • Use the B&Q kitchen planning service: For complex kitchens, B&Q offer an in-store planning service. Take your floor plan (exported from our planner) to make the appointment more productive.
  • Allow a contingency budget: Unexpected costs are common in kitchen renovations — set aside at least 10–15% of your budget for surprises.

  • Don't forget the electrics: Under-cabinet lighting, additional sockets, and extractor fan wiring all need to be planned before the units go in.

Frequently Asked Questions

What sizes do B&Q kitchen units come in?

B&Q kitchen units are available in a range of standard widths, typically from 150mm to 1000mm for base units, with the most common widths being 300mm, 400mm, 500mm, 600mm, and 800mm. Wall units generally mirror these widths, while heights vary depending on whether you choose standard, tall, or wall-height cabinets. Always check the specific dimensions on the B&Q website, as sizing can vary between ranges.

How accurate does my kitchen plan need to be when ordering B&Q units?

Very accurate. Kitchen units need to fit together precisely, and even small errors — 5 to 10mm — can create gaps or prevent a unit from fitting at all. Always measure your room at least twice, account for filler panels and end panels in your measurements, and use a planning tool with snapping and live measurements to keep your layout precise.

Do I need a professional to plan a kitchen with B&Q units?

Not necessarily. Many homeowners successfully plan and even install their own kitchens using B&Q's modular unit system. However, for complex kitchens involving structural changes, gas appliances, or significant electrical work, involving a professional is strongly advised. Even if you plan the layout yourself, having a qualified kitchen fitter or joiner carry out the installation can save significant time and ensure a high-quality result.
