How to Plan a Single-Storey Extension: A Homeowner's Step-by-Step Guide

March 2026 5 min read Try Free Room Planner free

You have decided you need more space. You are past the "should we move or should we extend" conversation. Now the question is how to plan the extension properly — before engaging an architect, before approaching a builder, and before spending money on anything you cannot reverse. Here is how to work through it step by step.

Permitted development versus planning permission

In England, most single-storey rear extensions can be built under Permitted Development — meaning you do not need to submit a planning application. The key limits are: 3 metres depth for terraced and semi-detached houses (or up to 6 metres under the Larger Home Extension scheme with prior approval), and 4 metres for detached houses (or up to 8 metres under the scheme). Maximum eaves height of 3 metres if within 2 metres of a boundary.

These are the default rules. Conservation areas, listed buildings, and properties subject to Article 4 directions may have tighter restrictions. Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland each have their own permitted development rules that differ from England's — check with your local planning authority before assuming any of these thresholds apply.

If your extension exceeds the PD limits, you will need a full planning application. This typically takes eight weeks and involves a fee, drawings, and sometimes a design and access statement.

What to work out before you engage an architect

An architect will design the extension. But they need a starting brief from you. Before the first meeting, work out three things:

  • What the room will do: Is it a kitchen extension, a living room, a kitchen-diner? The function determines the layout
  • How it connects to the existing house: Will you knock through entirely, or keep a doorway? Where does the new room meet the old one?
  • Where the light comes from: Which direction does the extension face? South-facing gets direct sun. North-facing needs more glazing to feel bright. This affects window and door positions

The rear extension versus the side return

A rear extension pushes the back wall of the house further into the garden. You gain floor space but lose outdoor space — and the rooms behind the extension may lose natural light.

A side return extension fills in the narrow passage alongside the house (common in Victorian terraces). It is usually cheaper per square metre because the footprint is smaller, and it does not reduce the garden. The trade-off is that a side return adds width to an existing room rather than creating a new one — useful for expanding a kitchen, less useful if you need a separate room.

Some projects combine both: a rear extension with a side return infill. This gives the most space but is more complex structurally and may require planning permission.

Party wall considerations

If your extension is built on or near the boundary with a neighbour, the Party Wall Act 1996 may apply. You are required to serve notice on your neighbour at least two months before work starts. If they consent, you can proceed. If they dissent, both parties appoint surveyors to agree a Party Wall Award — a document that records the condition of the neighbour's property and sets out how the work will be carried out.

This is not optional. Failing to serve notice can lead to injunctions and delays. Most neighbours consent without issue, but budget two to three months for the process and a surveyor fee of around £700 to £1,500 per neighbour if an award is needed.

Drawing what you want before the architect visit

You do not need to produce architectural drawings. You need to produce a sketch that shows what you are imagining: the rough footprint of the extension, how it connects to the house, where you think the doors and windows should go, and what the internal layout might look like.

This gives the architect something to react to. Without it, the first meeting is spent on questions you could have answered in advance. With it, the architect can immediately say "this works," "this won't work because of the drainage run," or "have you considered this alternative." The conversation starts at a higher level.

What the architect will challenge — and why that is useful

Expect the architect to push back on parts of your sketch. They may say the window is too close to the boundary. They may say the structural opening you want is wider than a standard steel can span economically. They may suggest a layout you had not considered.

This is the value of the process. You are not paying the architect to draw what you have already drawn. You are paying them to apply structural knowledge, planning knowledge, and spatial design experience to improve your idea. The sketch gives them a starting point. Their expertise gives you a better result.

The step most homeowners skip: testing the internal layout before construction

Most homeowners focus on the external footprint — how far the extension projects, where the roof line sits, what the planning limits allow. The internal layout gets decided later, sometimes after construction has started. This is a mistake.

The internal layout determines whether the extension actually works as a room. Where does the dining table go? Is there enough space for kitchen units on both sides? Can you walk from the back door to the living room without passing through the cooking area? These questions need answers before the slab is poured, not after.

Draw the internal layout to scale. Place the furniture. Check the clearances. If the layout does not work on paper, it will not work in concrete either.

Sketch the layout of your extension before you meet your architect. It gives them something to react to and saves the first meeting.
