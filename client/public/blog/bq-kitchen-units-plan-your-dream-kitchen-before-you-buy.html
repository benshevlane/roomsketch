Why Planning Your Kitchen Layout Matters So Much

It's tempting to walk into B&Q, fall in love with a door style, and start adding units to your trolley. But kitchens are complex spaces where the relationship between your cooker, sink, worktop, and storage can make or break the room's functionality. A poorly planned kitchen leads to awkward corner units, doors that clash when opened at the same time, and — worst of all — units that simply don't fit.

Kitchen fitters and contractors see this happen all the time. Homeowners arrive with a rough idea and a list of units, only to discover on installation day that a 600mm base unit is blocking a radiator, or that there isn't enough clearance for the dishwasher door to open fully. Planning your layout digitally, before anything is purchased, eliminates these costly surprises.

Understanding B&Q Kitchen Unit Sizes

Before you start designing, it helps to understand the standard dimensions that B&Q (and most UK kitchen retailers) use. This makes it far easier to plan accurately using a digital tool.

Base Units

  • Standard height: 720mm (excluding worktop)
  • Standard depth: 560–580mm
  • Widths available: typically 300mm, 400mm, 500mm, 600mm, 800mm, and 1000mm

Wall Units

  • Standard height: 720mm or 900mm
  • Standard depth: 300mm
  • Widths available: 300mm, 400mm, 500mm, 600mm, and 1000mm

Tall Units (Larder/Oven Housing)

  • Standard height: 1970mm or 2150mm
  • Standard depth: 560–580mm
  • Widths available: 300mm, 600mm

The Most Popular Kitchen Layouts and How to Choose Yours

Before you browse B&Q kitchen units, decide which layout best suits your space. The layout determines how many and which types of units you'll need.

Galley Kitchen

Two parallel runs of units facing each other. Works brilliantly in narrow rooms and is highly efficient for cooking. You'll typically need two rows of base units and matching wall units. Make sure you leave at least 1.2 metres between the facing runs for comfortable movement.

L-Shaped Kitchen

One of the most common layouts in UK homes. Units run along two adjoining walls, often incorporating a corner unit. B&Q offer various corner solutions including carousel units and Le Mans pull-outs to maximise that awkward corner space.

U-Shaped Kitchen

Units on three walls, offering maximum storage and worktop space. Ideal for larger kitchens. Requires careful planning around the two corners involved. You'll need at least 2.4 metres of clear space in the centre to move comfortably.

Single-Wall Kitchen

All units along one wall. Common in open-plan spaces and studio flats. Simple to plan, but storage can feel limited — consider tall larder units to compensate.

Kitchen with Island

Any of the above layouts with a freestanding or fixed island unit added. Islands require a minimum of 1.0–1.2 metres of clearance on all sides. B&Q sell island base units designed specifically for this purpose.

  1. Measure your kitchen accurately. Use a tape measure to record the length and width of your room, plus the position of doors, windows, radiators, and any fixed utilities (boiler, gas point, soil pipe). Write everything down in centimetres.

Tips for Getting the Most Out of B&Q Kitchen Units

Once your layout is mapped out, here are some practical tips to make the most of what B&Q have to offer.

Mix and Match Thoughtfully

B&Q allow you to mix base and wall unit carcasses from their core range with different door fronts. This means you can choose a budget carcass and invest more in a premium door style — a smart way to control costs without compromising on appearance.

Don't Forget the Fillers and Panels

End panels, plinths, cornices, and pelmet strips all add to the final unit count and cost. When planning, make sure you account for these finishing pieces, especially on any unit that will be exposed at the end of a run.

Consider Soft-Close Hinges and Drawer Runners

B&Q offer soft-close upgrades on many of their units. These are worth the investment — especially in a busy family kitchen. Factor in whether these are included as standard or whether they need to be specified separately.

Think About Lighting

Under-cabinet LED lighting can transform the feel of a kitchen. Plan for this during the layout stage by ensuring there's a suitable power source above your base unit runs.

Check Lead Times

B&Q kitchen units are often in stock in-store, but specific finishes, sizes, or accessories may need to be ordered. Build a realistic timeline into your project — especially if you're coordinating with a fitter.

Frequently Asked Questions

What standard sizes do B&Q kitchen units come in?

B&Q kitchen base units are typically 720mm tall (excluding worktop), 560–580mm deep, and available in widths of 300mm, 400mm, 500mm, 600mm, 800mm, and 1000mm. Wall units are usually 300mm deep and come in heights of 720mm or 900mm. Tall larder and oven housing units are typically 1970mm or 2150mm tall. These standard dimensions make it straightforward to plan your layout digitally using a grid-based floor planner.

How do I know if my kitchen units will fit before I buy?

The best way to check is to draw your kitchen to scale using a digital floor planner. Do I need a professional kitchen designer to plan B&Q units?

Not necessarily. B&Q offer their own in-store and online design service, which can be very useful. However, many homeowners successfully plan their own kitchens using free tools like ours, then either self-install or hand the plan to a fitter. Having your own pre-drawn layout can also make a paid design consultation much more efficient, as the designer can build on your existing ideas rather than starting from scratch.

What's the minimum clearance needed in a kitchen?

UK building regulations and kitchen design best practice recommend a minimum of 1.0 metre between facing runs of units, though 1.2 metres is preferable for comfortable movement and to allow appliance doors to open fully. For kitchens with an island, allow at least 1.0–1.2 metres of clear walkway on all sides of the island.
