10 Small Bedroom Layout Ideas That Make the Most of Limited Space

March 2026 4 min read Try Free Room Planner free

Most small bedroom advice focuses on style — light colours, clever storage, mirrors. That is all fine, but it skips the actual constraint. The problem with a small bedroom is not how it looks. It is whether you can walk around the bed, open the wardrobe, and get dressed without performing a choreographed routine every morning. The layout is what fixes this.

1. The bed against the longest wall

The most common starting point. Centering the bed on the longest wall gives you the most remaining floor space and usually allows access from both sides. If the longest wall has a radiator, you may need to offset the bed — but this is still the first layout worth trying in any small room.

2. The bed against the wall — single side access

Pushing one side of the bed flush against a wall frees the entire opposite side of the room. You lose bedside access on one side, but you gain enough floor space for a desk, a wardrobe, or a clear walking route. This works best for single-occupant rooms and children's bedrooms where floor space for play matters more than symmetrical access.

3. Bedhead against the window

People instinctively avoid this because of draughts, light, and the idea that it "looks wrong." In practice, with a decent blind and modern double glazing, it works perfectly well — and it frees the opposite wall for a full-width wardrobe or storage run. If the window wall is the shortest wall in the room, this layout can transform the usable space.

4. Using the alcoves

If your room has a chimney breast, the alcoves on either side are typically 35 to 45 centimetres deep. That is too shallow for hanging clothes, but deep enough for shelving, folded storage, or a narrow desk. Built-in shelving in the alcoves and the bed centred on the chimney breast wall is one of the most space-efficient layouts in older UK homes. It uses dead space that a freestanding wardrobe cannot reach.

5. Built-in wardrobes versus freestanding

A freestanding wardrobe is typically 60 centimetres deep. A built-in wardrobe along the same wall can be designed to exactly the depth you need — and it runs floor to ceiling, using vertical space that a freestanding unit wastes. The spatial difference is significant: a built-in across a 2-metre wall gives you roughly the same hanging space as two freestanding wardrobes while projecting less into the room. Sliding doors rather than hinged ones eliminate the swing clearance entirely.

6. Beds with storage underneath

Ottoman beds and divan beds with drawers use the dead space beneath the mattress for bedding, clothing, and seasonal items. Before committing, check two things on your floor plan: whether the ottoman lift mechanism has enough clearance to open fully (you need roughly 40 centimetres above the mattress top), and whether drawer beds have enough floor clearance on the drawer side to pull them out.

7. Positioning the door — when the swing eats the room

A standard bedroom door swings 90 degrees into the room. In a small bedroom, that arc can consume the only viable position for a bedside table or wardrobe. Consider whether the door can be rehung to open outward, or replaced with a sliding pocket door. Removing the door swing from the floor plan frees a surprisingly large area.

8. The loft hatch problem

If the loft hatch is in the bedroom ceiling, you need to keep the area beneath it clear enough to set up a ladder. This is an invisible constraint — it does not show on most floor plans but it dictates where tall furniture can go. Mark the loft hatch position on your plan and draw a clearance zone beneath it before finalising the layout.

9. Mirrored furniture and light

Mirrored wardrobe doors genuinely make a small room feel larger. This is not a design myth — a floor-to-ceiling mirror on the wardrobe reflects the window light and visually doubles the depth of the room. The effect is real and significant. What is overstated is the idea that paint colour or small decorative mirrors achieve the same result. They do not. Scale matters.

10. Drawing the room to scale before moving anything

The single most useful thing you can do with a small bedroom is draw it to scale and try every layout on paper before touching the furniture. Moving a bed takes two people and twenty minutes. Moving a bed on a floor plan takes two seconds. Try the bed on every wall. Try the wardrobe in every position. Check the clearances. Find the layout that works before you commit to it physically.
