## General Questions

### What is a room planner?

A room planner is a digital tool that lets you draw your room to scale and arrange furniture within it on screen. Instead of moving heavy furniture around and hoping something works, you can try unlimited layouts in minutes — for free, without any physical effort.

You enter your room's real measurements, add furniture pieces (sized to match your actual furniture), and arrange them until you find a layout that works. Because everything is to scale, when something fits in the plan, it fits in real life.

## Measuring and Accuracy

### How accurate do my measurements need to be?

For furniture layout planning, measuring to within 2–3cm is perfectly adequate. This level of accuracy is easy to achieve with a standard tape measure and gives you reliable results for most planning decisions — whether a sofa fits, whether there's room to walk around the bed, whether the dining table allows enough space on all sides.

For more precise applications — like planning a fitted kitchen, a built-in wardrobe, or any furniture that needs to fit exactly — aim for within 1cm. In these cases, measure each dimension at least twice to confirm.

### What do I need to measure?

At minimum: the length and width of the room; the position and width of each door (and which way it opens); the position and width of each window.

Optionally but usefully: the position of radiators and sockets (useful for furniture placement decisions); the height of the room (relevant for tall wardrobes or shelving); the depth and width of any alcoves or chimney breast projections.

### My room isn't a perfect rectangle — can I still use a room planner?

Yes. Free Room Planner can represent rooms of irregular shapes. For L-shaped rooms, plan each rectangular section and note how they connect. For rooms with alcoves or chimney breasts, measure these features separately and include them as projections in the plan. For rooms with angled walls (common in loft conversions), mark the wall as a constraint and plan around it.

### I don't have a tape measure — can I still get useful results?

You can get a rough plan using estimated dimensions, but the results won't be reliable enough to make real furniture decisions. A 5-metre tape measure costs about £5 at any hardware or DIY shop and is worth buying. Alternatively, many smartphones have a measure app that uses the camera for distances — these aren't as accurate as a tape measure but are better than estimating.

## Using the Planner

### How do I add furniture to my room plan?

Select the furniture type from the library — sofas, beds, dining tables, wardrobes, desks, and many more. Drag it into your room. Resize it to match your actual furniture dimensions by adjusting the width and depth. Rotate it to the angle you want. That's the whole process — it takes a few seconds per piece once you've done it once.

### Can I enter my actual furniture dimensions?

Yes. Every furniture item in Free Room Planner can be resized to match your real furniture. Measure your actual sofa, bed, wardrobe — whatever you're planning — and enter those dimensions. This is the most important step for getting accurate results. Default furniture sizes in any planner are approximate; your actual furniture will have specific dimensions that might be quite different.

### Can I save my room plan and come back to it?

Yes. You can save your plan within the tool and return to it in a later session. You can also screenshot or export it to save outside the tool — useful for sharing with a partner, showing to a contractor, or keeping a reference image when you're furniture shopping.

### Can I plan multiple rooms in one session?

Yes. You can plan individual rooms separately or work on a whole-home layout depending on your project. Many people plan each room individually and then think about how the rooms connect.

## Practical Planning Questions

### What's the minimum walkway width I should maintain in any room?

The main walkway through any room — the primary route you use frequently — should be at least 90cm wide. Secondary routes (less used, such as the side of a bed that less frequently needs to be accessed) can be 60–70cm. These minimums feel comfortable in real life and align with standard accessibility guidelines.

### How do I plan a room I haven't moved into yet?

Get the room dimensions from the estate agent, landlord, or property listing — then verify them yourself as soon as you have access. Enter those dimensions in Free Room Planner and start planning before moving day. You'll arrive on the day already knowing where everything goes, which saves significant time, effort, and the chaos of making layout decisions while surrounded by boxes.

### I'm thinking of buying new furniture — how do I check it will fit before I buy?

Find the dimensions of the furniture you're considering (these are almost always listed on the retailer's product page or in the product specification). Open Free Room Planner, draw your room, and add a furniture item at those exact dimensions. You'll see immediately whether it fits with appropriate clearances around it, and whether it works with your other furniture.

This takes about five minutes and can save the significant hassle of returning a piece of furniture that doesn't fit. It's one of the most practically useful things a room planner can do.

### How do I know if my planned layout will actually feel good in real life?

Once you have a plan on paper (or screen), mentally walk through it. Imagine coming into the room from the door. Move to the sofa, the bed, the wardrobe. Check each journey: are the routes clear? Is there enough space to move comfortably? Are the things you use most often within easy reach?

Then try the same exercise for different times of day and different use cases: getting ready in the morning, sitting down in the evening, having people over. If any of these sequences encounters friction in your mental walkthrough, the layout needs adjusting.
