# Space Planning & Room Layout FAQ

Questions about space planning, living room arrangement, and making any room work better. Here are the answers.

---

## Living Room Layout Questions

### What size rug do I need for my living room?

The most widely used guideline: all the main seating pieces (sofa and chairs) should either **all sit on the rug**, or **all have just their front legs on it**. A rug where all the furniture is completely off it looks like a mistake and makes the room feel disjointed. A rug where all the furniture sits on it creates a cohesive, defined zone.

For a typical living room with a 3-seater sofa and two armchairs, **200cm x 250cm** is a useful minimum. **240cm x 300cm** is often better. When in doubt, go larger — a rug that's slightly too big is much better than one that's slightly too small.

### How far should the sofa be from the TV?

A practical guide: **1.5 to 2.5 times the diagonal screen size** of the TV. For a 55-inch TV (approximately 1.4m diagonal), aim for 2.1m to 3.5m between the sofa and the screen. For a 65-inch TV (approximately 1.65m diagonal), 2.5m to 4m.

In practice, most living rooms end up at **2–3m** between sofa and TV, which is comfortable for typical screen sizes and typical room proportions.

### My living room feels like a corridor — how do I fix the layout?

Long, narrow living rooms (common in Victorian terraced houses) need to be divided into zones across the width, not arranged in a single line along the length. The key moves:

- Float the sofa perpendicular to the long axis (across the width of the room) rather than along it
- Add a rug to define and anchor the seating zone
- Create a secondary use at the far end of the room — a reading corner, a small desk, a drinks trolley — so the room feels purposeful throughout its length rather than just at one end
- Avoid furniture that emphasises the length (tall, narrow pieces along the long wall)

### Should the sofa face the door or the window?

Neither facing the door nor facing the window is a rule. The sofa should face the **focal point** — the fireplace, TV, or feature window. As a secondary consideration, the main seating should have peripheral sight of the entrance — sitting with your back completely to the main door can feel uncomfortable over time, even if you can't articulate why.

### How do I make a small living room feel bigger?

Several approaches work well together:

- Pull furniture slightly away from the walls — floating furniture counterintuitively makes rooms feel more spacious
- Choose furniture properly scaled to the room (a 3-seater sofa in a small room is often wrong)
- Use a single large rug rather than multiple small ones
- Keep window areas completely clear of furniture to maximise natural light
- Use mirrors on walls opposite windows to reflect light and add visual depth
- Keep surfaces clear of clutter — visible clutter makes rooms feel smaller and less restful

## General Space Planning Questions

### What's the minimum walkway width in a room?

Primary walkways (routes you use frequently): minimum **90cm**. Secondary walkways (less-used routes, such as the far side of a bed or beside a guest chair): **60–70cm**. In a kitchen, between facing runs of units: minimum **120cm**. Fire exit routes must maintain at least **100cm** clearance.

### How do I plan an open-plan room?

Zone it. Use furniture arrangement, rugs, lighting, and colour to create defined areas within the open space. Each zone should have a clear purpose (living, dining, cooking) and feel distinct even though it shares the same floor and ceiling with the others.

The most important principle: avoid placing the sofa with its back to the kitchen. This creates a disconnected, unwelcoming dynamic in the living zone. Instead, angle the sofa so it can acknowledge both the living zone's focal point and the kitchen beyond it.

### What's the best way to create zones in a room?

From most to least impactful:

1. **Furniture arrangement** — a sofa facing a direction implicitly defines a zone
2. **Rugs** — a rug under the seating group visually anchors the living zone even in an open-plan space
3. **Lighting** — a pendant over the dining area marks the dining zone; different light fittings in different areas create different atmospheres
4. **Colour and material variation** — different paint colours or floor treatments in different areas (more common in new builds and renovations)
5. **Physical dividers** — bookshelves, room dividers, or half-walls create the most definitive zoning

### How do I plan furniture placement if I don't have the furniture yet?

Use standard size ranges as a starting point in your plan. A 3-seater sofa: plan for approximately 200–220cm wide. A king-size bed: 150cm x 200cm. A 4-person dining table: 90cm x 120–150cm. When you identify specific pieces you're considering buying, use their exact dimensions in your plan to confirm they work. ### My room has a chimney breast. How does this affect the layout?

A chimney breast creates two alcoves — one on each side — which are among the most useful architectural features in any UK living room or bedroom. Alcoves are ideal for built-in storage, shelving, a compact desk, or a TV. The chimney breast itself, especially if it has a working fireplace, is an automatic focal point for the seating arrangement. Embrace it rather than treating it as an obstacle.

### What's the difference between space planning and interior design?

Space planning is about the functional organisation of a room — where things go, how you move through the space, what activities each area supports. Interior design is broader: it includes all the aesthetic decisions (colour, material, lighting, texture, decoration) that determine how a space looks and feels, built on top of the functional foundation of space planning. You can do good space planning yourself with a free tool; interior design skills take longer to develop.

### How much furniture is too much?

A useful rule of thumb: furniture should occupy no more than **two-thirds of the floor space** in a room. The remaining third is negative space — walkways, visual breathing room, and the freedom to move. Rooms that feel cramped are usually rooms where this ratio has been violated, either with too many pieces or with pieces that are too large for the space.
