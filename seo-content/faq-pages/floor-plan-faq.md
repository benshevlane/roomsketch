# Floor Plan FAQ: Everything You Need to Know

Everything you've wanted to know about floor plans — what they are, how to make them, what the symbols mean, and the best free tools for creating them at home.

---

### What is a floor plan?

A floor plan is a bird's-eye view drawing of a room or building, shown as if you've sliced through the walls horizontally at around waist height and looked down from above. It shows the layout of walls, doors, windows, and furniture, all drawn to scale. Floor plans are used for planning room layouts, communicating designs to contractors, and understanding the structure of a space.

### What is a floor plan used for?

For homeowners, floor plans are used primarily for furniture layout planning (checking what fits and how it should be arranged), for communicating renovation plans to builders or designers, and for planning kitchens and bathrooms where every centimetre matters. They're also useful for insurance documentation, property sales, and letting agents' listings.

### What scale should I use for a floor plan?

For a single room: **1:50** is standard (1cm on paper represents 50cm in real life, so a 4m wall = 8cm on your drawing). For a whole floor of a house: **1:100**. Digital tools like Free Room Planner handle scale automatically — you enter real-world measurements and the tool scales everything correctly for you.

### What do the symbols on a floor plan mean?

The main floor plan symbols:

- **Thick solid lines:** exterior walls
- **Thinner solid lines:** interior walls
- **Gap in a wall with an arc:** a door (the arc shows which direction it opens — this is the swing clearance zone)
- **Thin double line across an opening:** a window
- **Rectangle with rounded corners:** a bath
- **Circle or oval:** a basin or kitchen sink
- **Rectangle with a small lever shape:** a toilet
- **Rectangle divided by diagonal lines:** a bed (standard representation)
- **Small square in a corner:** an electrical socket or light switch (optional level of detail)

### How long does it take to make a floor plan?

For a single room using a digital tool like Free Room Planner: 20–40 minutes including measuring the room, entering the dimensions, and placing furniture. The measuring is usually the slowest part; the digital drawing and arranging is fast. For a whole house: a few hours spread across one or two sessions.

### How accurate does a floor plan need to be?

For furniture layout: within 2–5cm is sufficient for most decisions. For fitted furniture, kitchen planning, or built-in storage where every centimetre matters: within 1cm. For architectural plans used by contractors, for planning applications, or for structural work: these need to be professionally measured and drawn to precise tolerances. A floor plan you've made in a room planner is not suitable for planning applications.

### What's the difference between a floor plan and a room plan?

The terms are often used interchangeably. Strictly speaking, a floor plan refers to one level of a building (a whole floor), while a room plan refers to a single room. But in everyday use, people say "floor plan" for both. When estate agents or property listings refer to a "floor plan," they usually mean a plan of one whole floor of the property.

### Do I need a floor plan for planning permission?

For planning applications in the UK, you typically need professionally drawn floor plans that meet specific requirements: drawn to scale, with orientation (north arrow), a scale bar, and other technical elements. A floor plan you've made in a room planner is not suitable for planning applications. Engage an architect or architectural technologist for plans intended for planning permission submissions.

### Can I make a floor plan of my whole house?

Yes. Measure each room and hallway, then map them relative to each other. The key is to measure systematically — ideally starting from the outside walls and working inward — so that all the rooms connect accurately when you draw them together. Digital tools make this easier because you can adjust individual elements without redrawing everything.

### What information should I include in a floor plan?

For home planning purposes: room dimensions, wall positions, door positions and swing directions, window positions, and furniture placement. Optionally: radiator positions, socket and light switch positions, and fixed features like kitchen units or bathroom fixtures. The more information you include, the more useful the plan will be for furniture shopping and contractor conversations.
