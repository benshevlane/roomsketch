# Kitchen & Bathroom Planner FAQ

Planning a kitchen or bathroom renovation? Here are answers to the questions we hear most often about kitchen and bathroom planning.

---

## Kitchen Planning Questions

### What is the kitchen work triangle?

The kitchen work triangle is the imaginary triangle connecting your three most-used work areas: the sink, the hob, and the refrigerator. Kitchen designers have used this principle for decades because it describes how cooks actually move during meal preparation — repeatedly between these three points. For an efficient kitchen, no single side of the triangle should be shorter than 1.2m or longer than 2.7m, and the total perimeter should be between 4m and 8m.

### What's the minimum clearance between kitchen units?

The minimum clearance between facing kitchen unit runs (for example, in a galley kitchen) is **120cm**. This allows one person to work comfortably and another to pass behind them. For a kitchen regularly used by two cooks simultaneously, 150cm is much more comfortable and will noticeably reduce daily friction.

### How much worktop space do I need?

As a minimum, aim for **4 metres** of total worktop run in a family kitchen. Specifically: at least 40cm of worktop on at least one side of the hob for a landing zone; at least 60cm beside the sink for draining and prep; and a dedicated continuous prep area of at least 80–100cm. An island adds significant worktop area even in a smaller kitchen.

### Can I plan my kitchen myself or should I use a kitchen designer?

For a straightforward kitchen replacement in the same layout with new units and appliances, planning it yourself with a good room planner is very achievable. For a complex reconfiguration, moving plumbing or gas, knocking through walls, or a high-budget bespoke kitchen, a professional kitchen designer's fee (typically £500–£2,000 for a full design service) is usually worthwhile — they can identify practical problems that are easy to overlook and have supplier relationships that often recover the cost.

### What appliance dimensions do I need to plan for?

Standard integrated appliance opening sizes (the cabinet space you need to allow):

- Dishwasher: 60cm wide x 85cm tall
- Washing machine: 60cm wide x 85cm tall
- Standard fridge-freezer: 60cm wide (or 55cm for slimline integrated models)
- Single oven: 60cm wide x 60cm tall
- Hob: 60cm wide (standard) or 70cm wide (wide variant)
- Built-in microwave: typically 60cm wide — check your specific model

Always verify with the actual product you're buying before finalising cabinetry. Dimensions vary between brands and models.

### How do I plan kitchen lighting?

Plan at least three levels of kitchen lighting: general overhead lighting (recessed LED downlights work well); task lighting under wall units illuminating the worktop below (LED strip lights are the most practical solution); and ambient lighting above wall units or a pendant over an island.

Task lighting is most effectively wired during a kitchen fit — surface-mounted cables are an afterthought that can look messy. Plan your task lighting positions on the floor plan and share them with your electrician before any first fix electrical work begins.

### What's the kitchen work zone approach?

In larger kitchens, a zone-based approach works well alongside the work triangle. The five zones: **Consumables** (fridge, freezer, pantry); **Prep** (ample worktop space); **Cooking** (hob, oven, microwave); **Washing** (sink, dishwasher); **Crockery and cutlery** (ideally positioned between the washing zone and the dining area). Designing around zones helps create a kitchen that works efficiently for your actual cooking habits.

## Bathroom Planning Questions

### What clearance does a toilet need?

**In front of the toilet:** minimum 600mm, ideally 700–750mm. This is the space for sitting down, standing up, and using the toilet comfortably. In practice, 600mm feels very tight — if you have any room to increase it to 700mm+, do so.

**Either side of the toilet:** minimum 200mm from the edge of the seat to any wall. This is the minimum to sit comfortably. The centre of the toilet pan should be at least 400mm from any wall (200mm + approximately 200mm for half the seat width).

### What's the minimum bathroom size for a bath, toilet, and basin?

The absolute minimum for a bathroom containing a standard bath (1700mm x 700mm), toilet, and basin is approximately **2.4m x 1.7m**. This is very tight — enough to comply with building regulations but not comfortable for daily use. A more workable minimum is **2.5m x 2.0m**. If your bathroom is smaller than these dimensions, you may need to look at shorter baths (1500mm), compact toilets, or a shower instead of a bath.

### What's the minimum size for a shower enclosure?

**800mm x 800mm** is the building regulation minimum for a shower enclosure. In practice, this feels genuinely small — showering in an 800mm enclosure requires careful choreography. **900mm x 900mm** is noticeably more comfortable. If space allows, **1000mm x 800mm** or larger (walk-in shower) is the ideal.

### What type of door works best for a small bathroom?

In a small bathroom, an inward-opening door is often impractical because the swing arc takes up a significant chunk of the usable floor area. Better options:

- **Outward-opening door:** the most common practical solution for small bathrooms — no swing clearance needed inside the room
- **Sliding door:** no swing clearance at all — the door slides along the wall. Requires wall space beside the doorway.
- **Pocket door:** slides into the wall cavity — completely invisible when open, takes no space at all. Requires the wall to be modified, so is best planned at the design stage or during renovation.

### Can I move my toilet?

Yes, but the cost and complexity depend significantly on how far you want to move it. Toilets connect to the soil pipe — a large waste pipe that runs through (or under) the floor and typically exits through an external wall. Moving the toilet a short distance along the same wall (so the waste pipe doesn't need major rerouting) typically costs £500–£1,500. Moving it to a completely different wall may require significant plumbing work and potentially structural changes to route the soil pipe — this can cost several thousand pounds.

This is why planning your bathroom layout with the existing soil pipe position in mind can save significant money.

### Do I need planning permission for a new bathroom?

In most cases, no. Adding or changing a bathroom within the existing footprint of a house is normally **permitted development** and doesn't require planning permission. However:

- If you're converting a room into a bathroom (especially a bedroom — this affects the property's bedroom count), building regulations approval is typically needed
- If the work is on a listed building, listed building consent is required for any significant internal changes
- Always check with your local council if you're unsure — getting it wrong is more expensive than getting it right

### What's the difference between a bathroom, an en suite, and a shower room?

- **Bathroom:** contains a bath (plus usually a toilet and basin)
- **Shower room:** contains a shower enclosure instead of a bath (plus toilet and basin)
- **En suite:** a bathroom or shower room directly accessible from a bedroom, rather than from a hallway or landing
- **WC or cloakroom:** toilet and basin only — no bath or shower

For UK property valuations and estate agent descriptions, a "bathroom" specifically implies a bath. A shower room without a bath is typically listed separately and is generally considered less desirable than a bathroom for resale purposes.
