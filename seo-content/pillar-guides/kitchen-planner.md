## Why Kitchen Planning Matters More Than Any Other Room

In most rooms, if you get the furniture layout wrong, you can move things around. In a kitchen, once the units are fitted, the plumbing is in, and the appliances are wired, you're committed. Repositioning a sink or changing where your hob sits is expensive and disruptive. This is why planning your kitchen layout carefully before any work begins is essential, not optional.

A kitchen planner forces you to think through every practical decision in advance: where does the food prep happen? How do you move between the fridge, hob, and sink? Is there enough worktop space? Do the cabinet doors open without hitting each other? These questions have specific answers, and it's much better to discover them during planning than after installation.

## The Four Classic Kitchen Layouts

Most kitchens fall into one of four basic layout types. Understanding which you're working with shapes every other decision.

### 1. Galley Kitchen

Two parallel runs of units facing each other, with a walkway between them. Galleys are extremely efficient for cooking — everything is within reach and the work triangle is tight. The key requirement is a minimum of 120cm between the two runs (ideally 130–150cm for two people working together). Galley kitchens can feel narrow if they're too long; the ideal length is 3–5 metres.

### 2. L-Shaped Kitchen

Units along two adjacent walls, forming an L. This is one of the most versatile layouts — it works in small and medium kitchens, it creates a natural work triangle, and it leaves one end of the room open for a dining table or island. The corner is the trickiest part: corner base units need either a carousel or pull-out system to be usable.

### 3. U-Shaped Kitchen

Units on three walls, surrounding the cook on three sides. U-shaped kitchens maximise storage and worktop space, and the triple-wall layout means you can have clearly defined zones (cooking, prep, washing up) without walking across the room. The main limitation is that you need at least 2.4m between the two parallel runs to avoid the space feeling claustrophobic.

### 4. Kitchen with Island

Any of the above layouts plus a freestanding island unit in the centre. Islands are popular because they add prep space, storage, and a social focal point. The non-negotiable requirement: at least 100cm of clearance between the island and any wall unit or appliance on every walkable side. Less than this and the island becomes an obstruction rather than an asset.

## The Kitchen Work Triangle

The work triangle is the classic principle of kitchen design. The three most-used elements in any kitchen are the sink, the hob, and the refrigerator. The triangle is formed by imaginary lines connecting these three points. For a kitchen to work efficiently:

- No single side of the triangle should be shorter than 1.2m or longer than 2.7m
- The total perimeter of the triangle should be between 4m and 8m
- No major traffic route should cross through the triangle

When you use Free Room Planner to lay out your kitchen, place your sink, hob, and fridge first. Check the distances between them. If the triangle is too large, the kitchen will feel tiring to work in; if it's too small, it can feel cramped when more than one person is cooking.

In modern open-plan kitchens, the work triangle principle has evolved slightly to accommodate islands and multi-cook layouts — but the underlying idea (keep the key working elements close and unobstructed) remains as valid as ever.

## Kitchen Zones: A More Modern Approach

For larger kitchens, a zone-based approach works well alongside the work triangle:

- **Consumables zone:** fridge, freezer, pantry — where food is stored
- **Prep zone:** ample worktop space, near the consumables zone
- **Cooking zone:** hob, oven, microwave
- **Washing zone:** sink, dishwasher
- **Crockery and cutlery zone:** ideally between the washing zone and the dining area

Thinking in zones helps you design a kitchen that works for your actual cooking habits, not just a theoretical triangle.

## How to Measure Your Kitchen for Planning

Accurate kitchen measurement is critical. Unlike a bedroom where a few centimetres don't really matter, in a kitchen, 5cm can make the difference between a unit that fits and one that doesn't.

### What to Measure

- The full length of each wall, measured at floor level, worktop height, and wall unit height (walls aren't always perfectly straight)
- Every door and window: position from the nearest corner, width, and which way doors open
- All fixed utilities: the position of the soil pipe, waste pipe, gas supply, and any other services that can't be easily moved
- The position of any existing electrical sockets
- The ceiling height, and any beams or sloping sections
- Any radiator, particularly if it's on a wall you want to run units along

### Standard Appliance Dimensions

- Standard washing machine or dishwasher: 60cm wide x 85cm tall
- Standard fridge-freezer: 60cm wide (freestanding) or 55cm (integrated)
- Standard hob: 60cm or 70cm wide
- Standard oven: 60cm wide x 60cm tall (single) — allow for surrounding cabinetry

If you're fitting integrated appliances, plan the cabinet widths around them. If you're keeping existing appliances, measure them precisely.

## Kitchen Clearances: What You Need to Know

Adequate clearance makes a kitchen pleasant to work in; insufficient clearance makes it a daily frustration:

- Between parallel runs of units: minimum 120cm (ideally 130cm+; 150cm if two people cook together regularly)
- In front of an island: minimum 100cm on each walkable side
- Fridge door swing: check the door clearance before choosing a fridge (left or right hinge, or French door) and positioning it
- Oven door when open: approximately 50cm clearance in front to pull out a shelf safely
- Dishwasher door when open: approximately 50cm clearance — and you should be able to stand at the open dishwasher without blocking the sink or hob

The ones people most commonly overlook are the fridge swing (especially problematic in galley kitchens) and the dishwasher door (which often blocks the main kitchen walkway when open).

## How Much Worktop Space Do You Actually Need?

Most kitchens have too little worktop space. The UK average is around 3–4 metres of run; professional kitchen designers recommend at least 4–6 metres for a family kitchen. Specifically:

- At least 40cm of worktop beside the hob (on each side if possible) for a landing zone
- At least 60cm beside the sink for draining and washing up
- A dedicated prep area of at least 80–100cm uninterrupted worktop

When planning your layout, count the worktop runs carefully. An island adds significant worktop area even in a smaller kitchen.

## Kitchen Planning FAQ

### What's the minimum kitchen size for a workable layout?

A galley kitchen can work in as little as 2.4m wide x 3m long, though 3m x 4m is much more comfortable. For an L-shaped kitchen, you need at least two walls of 2m each. Below these minimums, you're looking at a very compact kitchen where every centimetre matters.

### Should I plan my kitchen myself or hire a designer?

For a straightforward kitchen replacement (same layout, new units), planning it yourself with a good room planner is very achievable. For a complex reconfiguration, moving plumbing, or a large bespoke kitchen, a professional kitchen designer's fee is usually worthwhile — they can spot practical problems that are easy to miss.

### Does kitchen lighting need to be planned at the floor plan stage?

Ideally, yes. Underunit task lighting is most effective when it's wired during a kitchen fit. If you're doing a full kitchen project, mark your planned lighting positions on the floor plan and share them with your electrician before any first fix work begins.

### Can I use Free Room Planner for an open-plan kitchen-diner?

Yes. Enter the full dimensions of the open-plan space and plan both zones simultaneously. This is particularly useful for ensuring the dining furniture doesn't intrude on the kitchen working areas and that any island relates well to both zones.

### How do I plan for a kitchen that two people cook in together?

Increase the clearance between facing runs to 150cm minimum. Try to create two distinct prep zones so two people can work simultaneously without getting in each other's way. Check the work triangle — in a two-cook kitchen, there are often effectively two triangles overlapping, and they should complement rather than conflict with each other.
