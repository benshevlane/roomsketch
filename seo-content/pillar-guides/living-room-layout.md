# Living Room Layout: The Complete Guide to Arranging Your Living Room

The living room is the heart of most homes — the place where you unwind after work, entertain friends, watch films on a rainy Sunday, and spend time with family. Getting the layout right matters more here than in almost any other room, because the wrong arrangement doesn't just look off — it makes the room feel uncomfortable and the daily experience of using it feels slightly wrong, even if you can't quite put your finger on why.

## Start With the Focal Point

The single most important decision in any living room layout is identifying the focal point — the dominant feature that anchors the arrangement and towards which the seating faces. In most UK living rooms, the focal point is one of:

- A fireplace or chimney breast
- A TV on a wall or media unit
- A picture window with a view
- An architectural feature like an exposed brick wall or a vaulted ceiling

If you have a fireplace, that's your focal point. Everything else comes second, including the TV — though in practice, many people place the TV above or beside the fireplace to combine both. If you don't have a fireplace, the TV wall becomes the focal point for most people.

Once you've identified the focal point, the sofa goes opposite it — or angled towards it. The seating arrangement builds around that relationship. Everything else — side tables, coffee table, armchairs, lamps — supports the primary seating arrangement rather than competing with it.

## The Most Common Living Room Layout Mistakes

### 1. Pushing Everything Against the Walls

This is the most universal living room mistake. When all the furniture is pushed against the walls, the centre of the room becomes an empty void, and the seating arrangement doesn't feel connected or intimate.

The solution is to float the furniture — pull the sofa away from the wall by 20–30cm, and arrange the seating group so it faces inward. Even moving the sofa a small distance from the wall makes a living room feel cosier and more intentional.

### 2. Choosing a Sofa That's Too Big or Too Small

A sofa that dominates the room makes everything feel cramped. A sofa that's too small for the space looks lost and provides inadequate seating. As a general guide:

- Small living room (under 15 sq m): a 2–2.5 seater sofa is usually right
- Medium living room (15–25 sq m): a 3-seater sofa, or a 2-seater plus armchair
- Large living room (25+ sq m): a 3-seater plus armchair(s), or a corner sofa

### 3. TV Positioned Too High

Mounting a TV too high on the wall — above eye level when seated — causes neck strain during extended viewing. The ideal TV height puts the centre of the screen at approximately seated eye level, which for most people is around 100–120cm from the floor.

### 4. Ignoring Traffic Flow

The living room needs clear routes — to the door, to the kitchen if adjacent, through to the garden. Furniture that blocks these routes creates constant friction. The main walkway through the living room should be at least 90cm wide.

### 5. Not Planning for Multiple Uses

Most living rooms need to function in multiple modes: relaxed solo viewing, active social gathering, possibly dining, possibly working from home. Plan the layout so it works across all the ways you use the room, not just for one mode.

## Living Room Layout Ideas for Every Shape and Size

### Small Living Room (Under 15 sq m)

Small living rooms require decisiveness and restraint:

- Choose a sofa and one armchair (or just the sofa) rather than trying to fit two sofas
- Use a coffee table that can be tucked under the sofa or is a small footstool rather than a large table
- Mount the TV on the wall rather than using a large media unit
- Use a single large rug rather than multiple small ones
- Mirrors and glass furniture help the room feel larger
- Built-in alcove shelving is the most efficient storage solution

In a small room, the most important thing is leaving clear floor space. Two pieces of furniture that work beautifully are worth more than four pieces that crowd the room.

### Rectangular Living Room

Long, narrow living rooms are common in Victorian terraced houses and they present a real layout challenge. The key is to divide the length into zones rather than trying to arrange everything along the long axis.

A classic approach: place the sofa near the middle of the room, floating perpendicular to the long axis, creating a seating zone towards one end (near the fireplace or TV) and a secondary zone at the other end (a reading corner, small desk, or dining area). A rug under each zone reinforces the separation.

### Square Living Room

Square rooms are easier to work with than narrow rectangles. A 4m x 4m room can comfortably hold a 3-seater sofa, two armchairs, a coffee table, and a TV unit without feeling cramped.

In a square room, try angling furniture at 45 degrees from the walls for a more dynamic arrangement. Or use a large corner sofa to make the most of two walls while keeping the opposite corner of the room open and airy.

### Open-Plan Living Room

Open-plan spaces combine the living room with a kitchen and/or dining area in one continuous space. Space planning principles are especially important here because poorly arranged open-plan rooms feel chaotic.

Use defined zones — a sofa arrangement with a rug for the living zone, dining table and chairs for the dining zone. The key is to ensure each zone feels distinct but the whole space flows coherently. Avoid placing the sofa with its back to the kitchen — it creates an uninviting dynamic.

## Sofa Arrangements: The Main Options

### One Sofa + One or Two Armchairs

The classic configuration. Works in almost any size room. The sofa faces the focal point, armchairs are positioned at angles or flanking the sofa. This creates an easy conversation arrangement and feels welcoming without being too formal.

### Two Sofas Facing Each Other

Creates a more formal, symmetrical arrangement that works well for rooms primarily used for entertaining. Requires a generous room (usually 4m+ wide) to avoid feeling confrontational. Works best when the facing sofas are separated by a coffee table with a clear walkway on either side.

### Corner Sofa

Corner sofas are excellent in medium to large rooms and provide plenty of seating in a compact footprint. The key is ensuring the corner of the sofa doesn't block a walkway, and that the TV is positioned so all seating has a reasonable view.

### U-Shape Arrangement

A sofa plus two flanking chairs facing the sofa. Creates a very intimate conversation group that feels enveloping and social. Requires a fairly large room (usually 5m x 4m minimum) and leaves little room for a coffee table in the centre. Suits a room primarily used for conversation rather than TV viewing.

## Living Room Layout FAQ

### How far should the sofa be from the TV?

A common guide is 1.5 to 2.5 times the diagonal screen size of the TV. For a 55-inch TV (about 1.4m diagonal), that's approximately 2.1–3.5m. For a 65-inch TV (about 1.65m diagonal), 2.5–4m. For most living rooms, 2–3m between the sofa and the TV screen is comfortable for typical screen sizes.

### Should the sofa face the door?

Not necessarily — but the dominant seating should have a view of the main entrance. People feel more comfortable when they can see who enters a room. This doesn't mean the sofa needs to face the door directly; having it at an angle that gives peripheral sight of the entrance is usually sufficient.

### How do I make a long, narrow living room feel wider?

Float furniture away from the long walls; use horizontal lines in the layout (a long sofa placed width-ways rather than length-ways); add a mirror on one of the short end walls; use a large area rug to anchor the seating group. Avoid tall, narrow furniture that emphasises the room's length and narrowness.

### What size rug for a living room?

As a rule of thumb: all the main seating pieces (sofa and chairs) should either all sit on the rug, or all have just their front legs on it. A rug that's too small makes the room look disjointed. For most living rooms, a rug of at least 200cm x 250cm is appropriate; 240cm x 300cm is often better.

### Should I use a coffee table or an ottoman?

Both work well — the choice comes down to use. A coffee table is better for displaying items, resting drinks, and using as a surface. An ottoman is better for putting your feet up, provides storage (if hinged-lid), and is softer visually. An upholstered ottoman with a tray on top gives you the best of both.
