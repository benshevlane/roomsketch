## The Bed: Everything Starts Here

The bed is the largest piece of furniture in any bedroom and the element everything else relates to. Place it first — and place it with intention. The most effective bed positions:

**Centred on the wall opposite the door.** This is the classic and usually the best position. You see the bed when you enter the room; it becomes the focal point. There's equal space on both sides for bedside tables, which makes the room feel balanced and welcoming.

**Against a side wall.** Works in narrower rooms where there isn't enough width for the bed plus clearance on both sides against the main wall. One side of the bed will be against the wall — ensure the person who sleeps on that side is comfortable with climbing over or under, and that there's still at least 60cm on the open side.

**In an alcove.** A deeply recessed alcove can make an excellent bed position — it feels cosy and purposeful, and the alcove provides a natural architectural frame. Built-in storage either side of an alcove bed is one of the most efficient bedroom storage configurations.

### Positions to Avoid

- Directly under a window: cold in winter, light in the face at dawn, and draughts can disturb sleep
- Directly opposite a window facing east: sunrise glare directly onto the pillow
- Where the door opens directly onto the foot of the bed: feels abrupt and can be unsafe if someone opens the door quickly
- Where the bed is visible from the hallway through the open bedroom door: reduces the sense of privacy

## Bedroom Clearances

The clearances around the bed are non-negotiable for comfortable daily use. These might look small on a plan, but they make a huge difference to how a bedroom actually feels to live in.

- **Either side of the bed (for getting in and out):** minimum 60cm, ideally 75cm
- **At the foot of the bed:** minimum 90cm if there's a walkway or a wardrobe
- **In front of a hinged wardrobe:** minimum 90cm to open the doors fully without obstruction
- **In front of a sliding-door wardrobe:** minimum 75cm (no swing clearance needed, but you need space to stand and use it)
- **In front of a chest of drawers:** minimum 90cm to open all drawers fully
- **Around a dressing table:** minimum 75cm to sit at it comfortably

The most common bedroom planning mistake is prioritising a larger bed over maintaining these clearances. A king-size bed with inadequate clearance on both sides is a worse daily experience than a double bed with generous space to move around.

## Storage Planning in the Bedroom

Most bedrooms don't have enough storage. The consequences — clothes draped over chairs, bags on the floor, surfaces perpetually cluttered — affect both the room's appearance and your quality of sleep. Research consistently shows that visual clutter in the bedroom is associated with worse sleep quality and higher stress levels at home.

### Small Bedroom (Under 10 sq m)

In a small bedroom, built-in storage almost always outperforms freestanding. Key strategies:

- A floor-to-ceiling built-in wardrobe in the alcove or along one full wall maximises every cubic centimetre of storage without the visual bulk of a freestanding wardrobe
- Under-bed storage with drawers or a lift-up ottoman base is some of the most efficient storage available — close to where you need the clothes, completely out of sight
- Wall-mounted shelving above the door or around the window frame uses space that's often left dead
- Slimline bedside tables (less than 40cm deep) or wall-hung bedside shelves free up floor space compared to standard bedside tables

### Medium Bedroom (10–15 sq m)

A freestanding wardrobe or built-in plus a chest of drawers is usually achievable. Key strategies:

- Bedside tables with drawers rather than open shelves — visible surfaces tend to accumulate clutter
- A window seat with storage below if you have a bay window (one of the most useful and underused storage features in any bedroom)
- A small wardrobe in the en suite or dressing area if there's an adjacent space, freeing up the main bedroom

### Large Bedroom (15+ sq m)

In a larger bedroom, storage can be more expansive but shouldn't be excessive:

- A walk-in wardrobe or dressing room (if space and budget allow) keeps all clothing and dressing activity completely out of the sleeping space
- A dedicated dressing table in a window area creates a pleasant getting-ready ritual
- A seating area — an armchair or small sofa — works beautifully in a larger bedroom and adds a sense of luxury, provided all clearances are maintained throughout the rest of the room

## Natural Light and the Bedroom Layout

Natural light isn't just about aesthetics — it affects how easy it is to get up in the morning, how you perceive the room's colour scheme, and how well you sleep (blackout matters, but it matters even more to have good light management in the morning).

Where possible:

- Position your main dressing or getting-ready area in natural light — it makes getting dressed significantly easier and more accurate
- Avoid placing the bed in a position where direct sunlight hits the pillow if you're an early riser in an east-facing room
- Consider where the light will be at different times of day: morning light from the east, afternoon light from the west, north-facing rooms that are consistently softer and cooler

## Small Bedroom Planning Tips

In a small bedroom, every decision matters. Here's how to make a modest space feel generous:

**Keep furniture to the minimum.** Every piece needs to justify its presence. A bed with storage drawers removes the need for a chest of drawers. A built-in wardrobe removes the visual bulk of a freestanding one.

**Use mirrors strategically.** A large mirror on the wardrobe doors or a full-length mirror on one wall creates the impression of depth and reflects light throughout the room.

**Choose light, neutral wall colours.** Dark feature walls can look dramatic but they make small rooms feel smaller. Light, warm neutrals reflect more light and make a small bedroom feel more open.

**Keep the floor clear.** Visible floor space makes a room feel significantly larger than the same room with items on the floor. Good storage planning is what enables a clear floor — it's not just about tidying up.

**Floating bedside tables over freestanding.** A wall-hung bedside shelf takes up no floor space and creates a visual openness that a standard bedside table doesn't. In a small room, this small change makes a noticeable difference.

## Bedroom Layout for Two People

When two people share a bedroom, you're planning for two distinct daily routines that need to coexist without conflict:

- **Equal access on both sides of the bed** — minimum 60cm each side, regardless of room size. If one person regularly has to climb over the other to get in or out of bed, the layout needs rethinking.
- **Both should have a bedside table** — even if one is smaller than the other, both people need a surface for a phone, a lamp, and a glass of water.
- **The wardrobe should be accessible from both sides of the room** — or, ideally, each person should have their own storage allocation, clearly defined within the shared wardrobe.
- **Plan the getting-ready zone so two people can use it simultaneously** — two people trying to use the same mirror, the same socket, or the same bedside area at the same time every morning creates daily friction that adds up.

## Master Bedroom vs Secondary Bedroom

**Master bedrooms** can accommodate more furniture and storage, but more space doesn't automatically mean more furniture. A generously spaced master bedroom with a king-size bed, two bedside tables, and a single large wardrobe often feels better than one crammed with furniture. Restraint in a large bedroom creates a sense of luxury.

**Secondary bedrooms** (for children or guests) often need more flexible furniture: a single or small double bed that can serve as a sofa when not in use as a bedroom, or a loft bed with desk beneath for a child's room. A guest bedroom that also functions as a home office is a common configuration — a fold-down desk, good task lighting, and a high-quality sofa bed can make this work well.

## Bedroom Planner FAQ

### What size bed should I get for my room?

As a general guide: a king-size bed (150cm x 200cm) needs a room of at least 3.5m wide to have 60cm clearance on both sides plus space against the wall. A double (135cm x 190cm) works in rooms from about 3m wide. A single (90cm x 190cm) fits comfortably in rooms from about 2.4m wide. Use Free Room Planner to check the exact clearances for your specific room dimensions.

### How do I make a small bedroom feel bigger?

Keep furniture to the essentials. Use built-in storage to maximise the room's natural dimensions. Choose a bed with storage drawers to remove the need for a separate chest. Use mirrors. Keep the floor completely clear. Opt for light wall colours and good window treatments. Floating bedside shelves instead of freestanding tables also help.

### Should I put the bed against the wall?

Only if the room is too narrow to have clearance on both sides. Having both sides of the bed accessible is much more practical for daily use, and is especially important when two people share the room. A bed pushed against the wall is a last resort, not a design choice.

### Can I have a double bed in a small bedroom?

Often yes. A double bed (135cm x 190cm) in a 3m x 3.5m room leaves approximately 75cm each side if you centre it on the main wall — which is perfectly comfortable. Use Free Room Planner to check your specific dimensions before ruling out a larger bed.
