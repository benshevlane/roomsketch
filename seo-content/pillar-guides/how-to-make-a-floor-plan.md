## What Is a Floor Plan and Why Does It Matter?

A floor plan is a bird's-eye view drawing of a room or building, shown as if you've sliced through the walls horizontally at around waist height and looked down. It shows the shape and size of the space, the positions of walls, doors, and windows, and — for design purposes — where furniture and fixtures sit.

Floor plans matter for several practical reasons:

- They let you plan furniture layouts before buying or moving anything
- They help contractors understand your vision and quote accurately
- They make it easier to spot problems — like a door that opens into a sofa, or a bed that blocks the only window — before they happen in real life
- They're essential for planning kitchens, bathrooms, and built-in storage where dimensions are critical
- They give you a shared reference point when discussing plans with family, builders, or designers

Essentially, a floor plan turns the abstract ("I want to move the bed to the other wall") into something concrete and measurable. It removes guesswork.

## What You Need to Make a Floor Plan

### For Manual Drawing

- A tape measure (3–5 metres is ideal)
- Graph paper (5mm or 10mm squares work well)
- A sharp pencil, a ruler, and an eraser
- A notepad for recording measurements

Manual floor plans are drawn to a scale — typically 1:50 for room-level plans (1cm on paper = 50cm in real life) or 1:100 for whole-house plans. So a 4-metre wall would be drawn as 8cm on paper at 1:50 scale.

## Step 1: Measure the Room

Accurate measurements are the foundation of any good floor plan. This step is worth doing carefully — a 10cm error can mean the difference between a sofa that fits and one that doesn't.

### Tools for Measuring

A standard retractable tape measure works perfectly for rooms up to 5–6 metres. For larger spaces, a long tape (10m) makes things easier. Laser distance meters are fast and convenient, but be aware they measure to the face of the skirting board, not the wall behind it, which can cause small discrepancies.

### What to Measure

Go around the room systematically. Measure:

- The full length of each wall, from corner to corner
- Every door: the width of the door frame, its distance from the nearest corner, and which way it opens
- Every window: the width of the window reveal, its distance from each corner, and the height of the sill from the floor
- Any projections: chimney breasts, alcoves, pillars, boxing, or built-in furniture
- The position of any fixed radiators, boiler, or electrical consumer unit if relevant

Write each measurement on a rough sketch as you go. A quick pencil sketch with numbers scrawled on it is all you need — it doesn't have to be artistic, just accurate and complete.

### A Measuring Tip for Irregular Rooms

For odd-shaped rooms, it helps to think of them as a series of rectangles. Measure each rectangular section separately, then they'll join up when you draw the plan. An L-shaped room, for example, is two rectangles joined at a corner.

## Step 2: Draw the Outline — the Manual Method

If you're drawing by hand, here's how to turn your measurements into a scale drawing.

### Choose Your Scale

For a single room, 1:50 is ideal. At this scale:

- 1cm on paper = 50cm in real life
- A 3m wall = 6cm on paper
- A 5m wall = 10cm on paper
- A standard door (85cm wide) = 1.7cm on paper

### Draw the Walls

Start with the longest wall. Draw a straight line to scale along the bottom of your graph paper. Use the grid to keep things neat. Then work around the room clockwise, drawing each wall to scale from your measurements. Use a ruler throughout.

When you get back to your starting point, the lines should meet up. If they don't quite join, recheck your measurements — one of them is likely slightly off.

### Add Doors and Windows

Mark doors as an opening in the wall (a gap at the correct position and width) with a curved arc showing the swing direction. This arc is the clearance zone — you should never place furniture within it.

Windows are shown as a thin double line across the opening, representing the window frame within the wall thickness.

### Setting the Right Dimensions

Don't just use the default furniture sizes — measure your actual furniture (or the pieces you're planning to buy) and set those dimensions in the planner. Standard furniture sizes vary quite a bit between manufacturers, so using real measurements gives you a much more accurate picture.

### UK Standard Furniture Dimensions

**Beds:**
- Single: 90cm x 190cm
- Small double: 120cm x 190cm
- Double: 135cm x 190cm
- King: 150cm x 200cm
- Super king: 180cm x 200cm

**Sofas (approximate — varies hugely by model):**
- 2-seater: 140–170cm wide

- Corner sofa: 230–280cm on each side

**Dining tables:**
- For 2 people: 80cm x 80cm (square) or 80cm diameter (round)
- For 4 people: 90cm x 120–150cm
- For 6 people: 90cm x 180–200cm
- For 8 people: 90–100cm x 240–300cm

## Step 5: Check Clearances and Circulation

A floor plan that fits all the furniture isn't automatically a good floor plan. You also need to check that there are clear paths to move through the room, and that there's enough space around each piece of furniture to use it comfortably.

### Key Clearance Guidelines

- Primary walkways (main routes through the room): at least 90cm
- Secondary walkways (less-used routes): at least 60–70cm
- Bed clearance either side: at least 60cm (75cm is more comfortable)
- Sofa to coffee table: 40–50cm
- Around a dining table: at least 90cm between the edge of the table and any wall or other furniture
- In front of wardrobes and chest of drawers: at least 90cm to open without obstruction
- Kitchen work triangle sides: ideally 1.2m–2.7m each
- Bathroom in front of toilet: at least 60cm

Check each of these as you refine your plan. In Free Room Planner, you can measure distances directly on the canvas.

## Step 6: Iterate and Refine

The best floor plan is rarely the first one you draw. Treat the first version as a starting point, not a final answer. Try at least two or three different layouts before settling on one. Questions to ask yourself as you iterate:

- Does this layout make sense for how I actually use this room day-to-day?
- Is there a clear, unobstructed path through the room?
- Have I made good use of natural light — are key seating and working areas near windows?
- Is anything blocking a radiator or heat source?
- Do all the doors (including wardrobe doors) open fully without hitting anything?
- Will this furniture layout still work when I have guests?

## How to Design a Floor Plan for a Whole House

The same approach scales up to whole-house planning. If you're buying a new build, doing a major renovation, or planning a house extension, you'll want a floor plan of the whole property.

For a whole-house floor plan, measure every room as described above, then map them relative to each other. Pay attention to:

- The shared walls between rooms — these define the building's structure
- The position of the staircase and hallways — these are the connective tissue of the house
- External doors and any attached garage or outbuilding
- North orientation — important for understanding which rooms get morning or afternoon sun

A whole-house plan is particularly useful for kitchen-diners, open-plan layouts, and extensions, where changes in one area affect the whole floor plate.

## Floor Plan Symbols: Quick Reference

Whether you're drawing by hand or using a digital tool, floor plans use standard symbols. The main ones to know:

- **Solid thick lines:** exterior walls
- **Solid thinner lines:** interior walls
- **Gap in wall with arc:** door (arc shows swing direction)
- **Double thin lines across gap:** window
- **Dashed rectangle:** above-counter features (wall cabinets, mezzanine)
- **Rectangle with rounded corners:** bath
- **Circle or oval:** basin or sink
- **Small rectangle with lever shape:** toilet
- **Small square in corner:** socket or switch (optional level of detail)

### What's the easiest way to make a floor plan?

The easiest way is to use a free online tool like Free Room Planner. Measure your room, enter the dimensions, then drag and drop furniture. Most people have a usable floor plan within 20–30 minutes.

### What scale should I use for a floor plan?

For a single room, 1:50 is standard (1cm on paper = 50cm in real life). For a whole house or floor, use 1:100. Digital tools handle scale automatically — you just enter real-world measurements.

### How accurate does a floor plan need to be?

For furniture planning, within 2–5cm is fine. For built-in furniture or kitchen fitting, aim for within 1cm. For architectural plans used by contractors or planning permission, you need professionally measured and drawn plans.

### Can I make a floor plan without measuring?

Not accurately, no. Even if you have a property listing with approximate room sizes, these are often rounded. Taking your own measurements takes 15–20 minutes per room and makes a significant difference to the accuracy of your plan.
