# Restaurant Floor Plan: How to Design Your Restaurant Layout

A restaurant floor plan is one of the most commercially significant design decisions you'll make. Get it right, and you maximise covers, create a great guest experience, and give your team an efficient working environment. Get it wrong, and you'll have cramped diners, a chaotic service flow, and staff walking unnecessary miles every shift.

## Start With the Commercial Reality

The number of covers (seats) in your restaurant directly determines your revenue potential. Every extra table you can fit adds to your top line — but cramming tables too close together damages the guest experience and ultimately hurts your reputation and repeat business.

Industry benchmarks for table spacing:

- **Fine dining:** 1.5–2 sq m per diner
- **Casual dining:** 1.2–1.5 sq m per diner
- **Fast casual or cafe:** 1–1.2 sq m per diner

These are guide ranges. For your specific venue, factor in the room shape, fixed elements like pillars and service stations, and the kind of experience you want to create. A cramped fine dining room is far more damaging to the brand than a reasonably tight casual dining one.

---

## Service Flow: Non-Negotiable

In a restaurant, the floor plan must work for your service team as much as for your guests. Poor service flow leads to slow service, unhappy staff, and costly errors. The key service routes to plan:

**Kitchen to dining room.** This is the primary service corridor. It needs to be wide enough for two servers to pass each other with plates — minimum 1.2m, ideally 1.5m or wider in busy sections.

**Back of house access.** Dish returns, re-orders, and prep trips from the dining room to the kitchen should follow a predictable route. A well-positioned kitchen pass or service window minimises unnecessary back-and-forth.

**Bar to tables.** If you have a bar, the route from bar to tables is another high-traffic corridor. Avoid placing tables directly in this route — it creates constant interruption and slows service.

**Fire exits.** Every restaurant must have clear, unobstructed routes to fire exits. These take absolute priority over table placement and can never be compromised.

## Table Configuration Options

### Banquette Seating

Banquette seating — fixed bench seating along walls or dividers — is popular in casual dining venues because it maximises seating density, is comfortable for guests, and creates a sense of enclosure and intimacy. The standard banquette depth is 45–50cm, and tables are typically 70–75cm wide. Allow at least 45cm of clear space between the table edge and the nearest obstruction or aisle.

### Two and Four-Top Tables

A mix of 2-top and 4-top tables gives you flexibility. Two-tops can be pushed together for larger parties; four-tops serve 2 or 4 guests (2-person parties on four-tops is less efficient but often unavoidable during quiet periods). Having a majority of 4-tops gives you maximum flexibility across different party sizes.

### Round Tables

Round tables fit more diners in the same footprint than square or rectangular tables and create a more sociable feel. The trade-off is that they're harder to arrange in rows and can leave awkward gaps in a rectangular room. They work particularly well in open floor areas and private dining rooms.

## Zoning Your Restaurant

Most successful restaurants have distinct zones that serve different purposes:

**The bar area.** Ideally near the entrance for walk-ins and waiting diners. The bar should be visible from the entrance but separated enough from the dining room that bar noise doesn't overwhelm seated diners.

**The prime section.** The best seats in the house — usually the tables with the best views, most comfortable seating, or most ambient feel. These can be saved for special occasions or VIPs, or simply serve as the best impression guests get of the dining room.

**The practical section.** Quieter areas near the kitchen, at the edges of the room, or in corners. Good for large parties, set menus, or regulars who prefer a quieter spot.

**The private dining room.** If your venue allows, a separate room for private events provides a useful revenue stream and doesn't require changing the main floor plan for each event.

## Kitchen Placement in a Restaurant Floor Plan

The kitchen's position relative to the dining room fundamentally affects service efficiency. Key considerations:

- **The pass** (the service window between kitchen and dining room) should be as central as possible relative to the dining room — this minimises the average walking distance for servers
- **The kitchen** should ideally be at the back of the venue, not adjacent to the entrance (noise and cooking smells are better directed away from arriving guests)
- **Staff areas** — staff toilets, dry store, and refrigeration — should be accessible from the kitchen without staff having to enter the dining room

## Accessibility and Compliance

Restaurant floor plans must meet UK accessibility requirements under the Equality Act 2010:

- At least one accessible toilet
- Main route through the restaurant of at least 900mm wide
- At least some tables accessible by wheelchair users
- An accessible entrance (no steps, or a ramp with a gradient no steeper than 1:12)

Additionally, your floor plan must comply with fire safety regulations — check with your local fire safety officer and building control team for specific requirements for your venue size and configuration.

---

## Outdoor Seating

If your venue has outdoor seating, plan it with the same rigour as the interior. Key considerations:

- **The service route:** will servers need to pass through the indoor dining room to reach outdoor tables, or is there a direct route?
- **Weather protection:** canopies, parasols, or heaters all affect the space footprint and need to be factored into the plan
- **Licensing:** many outdoor seating arrangements require a pavement licence or a variation of your premises licence — check requirements with your local authority before finalising your plan

## Restaurant Floor Plan FAQ

### How many covers can I fit in my restaurant?

A rough calculation: total dining room floor area (sq m) divided by your target sq m per diner gives the theoretical maximum. Then subtract approximately 15–20% for service stations, the pass, and fixed furniture. For a casual dining restaurant in a 60 sq m dining room: 60 ÷ 1.35 = 44 theoretical maximum; minus 15% = approximately 37 covers.

### Do I need a professional to design my restaurant floor plan?

For a major new opening or a complex renovation, a hospitality designer or specialist brings experience of what works operationally and what doesn't. For a smaller cafe or restaurant where you're doing the fit-out yourself or with a general contractor, Free Room Planner plus the principles in this guide will take you a long way.

### What's the minimum aisle width between tables?

For diners to pull out their chairs and for a server to pass: 1.2m minimum between table edges. For two servers to pass each other: 1.5m. Fire exit routes must maintain at least 1m clearance.

### Should I use banquette seating or individual chairs?

Banquette seating maximises density and creates a warm atmosphere, but it's fixed — you can't easily reconfigure it for different party sizes. Individual chairs give you much more flexibility. Many successful restaurants combine both: banquettes along the walls for atmosphere and intimacy, individual tables in the centre for flexibility.
