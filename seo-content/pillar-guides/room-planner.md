## What Is a Room Planner?

A room planner is a digital tool that lets you draw your room to scale and arrange furniture within it — all on screen, before anything gets moved in real life. Think of it like a bird's-eye view of your space that you can rearrange as many times as you like without breaking a sweat.

Modern online room planners include pre-built furniture shapes — sofas, beds, dining tables, desks — that you can drag, rotate, resize, and snap into position. You enter your room's actual measurements, and everything scales accordingly, so what you see on screen reflects what will actually fit in your room.

This matters more than people realise. The number one room planning mistake DIY homeowners make is buying furniture before checking it fits. A room planner eliminates that risk entirely.

### 1. See What Fits Before You Buy

Every piece of furniture has dimensions. Your room has dimensions. Those two sets of numbers need to work together — and it's not always obvious until you see them in the same space. A room planner does the maths for you, visually.

If you're buying a new sofa, for example, you can drop it into your room plan and instantly see whether there's still room to walk to the TV, whether the coffee table fits in front of it, and whether the whole layout feels balanced. Do this before you buy, and you'll never have to awkwardly return a sofa because it blocks the radiator.

### 2. Experiment Freely and Quickly

In real life, rearranging furniture takes time, effort, and usually a bruised toe or two. In a room planner, you can try ten different layouts in ten minutes. A good room layout isn't just about fitting furniture — it's about how you move through the space. You want clear walkways (at least 90cm wide for main routes), enough space around the bed to get dressed comfortably, and a natural flow from one area to another. A top-down room plan makes these traffic patterns immediately visible.

## How to Use a Room Planner: Step-by-Step

Here's exactly how to get from a blank canvas to a finished room plan you're happy with.

### Step 1: Measure Your Room

This is the most important step, and it's the one people rush. Take a proper tape measure and measure:

- The width and length of the room
- The position and width of every door (and which way it opens)
- The position and width of every window
- Any alcoves, chimney breasts, or built-in features
- The height of the room if you're planning tall furniture or storage

Write these measurements down on a rough sketch as you go. It doesn't have to be pretty — just accurate. Even being 5cm out can make a difference when you're planning a fitted kitchen or trying to fit a king-size bed into a tight space.

### Step 2: Enter Your Room Dimensions

Open Free Room Planner in your browser. Enter the length and width of your room. The planner will draw the room outline for you, to scale, on the canvas. Add doors and windows in the correct positions — these are critical because you should never block a door swing with furniture, and natural light from a window affects where you want to place seating.

### Step 3: Add Your Furniture

Browse the furniture library and drag in the pieces you're working with. If you already own the furniture, resize each piece to match its actual dimensions. If you're planning what to buy, use standard sizes as a starting point and adjust later.

Some standard UK furniture dimensions to know:

- Single bed: 90cm x 190cm
- Double bed: 135cm x 190cm
- King-size bed: 150cm x 200cm
- Super king bed: 180cm x 200cm
- Standard 3-seater sofa: approximately 210–240cm wide
- Dining table for 4: typically 90cm x 150cm
- Dining table for 6: typically 90cm x 180–200cm

### Step 4: Try Different Layouts

Don't settle for the first arrangement that works. Try at least three different layouts before committing. Common experiments worth trying:

- Moving the main sofa to face a different wall
- Floating furniture away from walls (this often makes rooms feel larger)
- Shifting the bed to a corner vs centred on a wall
- Angling a sofa or chair to create a cosier conversation area

For each layout, mentally walk through the room. Where do you come in? Can you walk freely to each exit? Is there enough space around the bed? Can you open all the drawers and wardrobe doors?

### Step 5: Check Your Spacing

Good room design has recommended minimum spacing for comfort and practicality:

- Main walkways: at least 90cm wide
- Around a dining table: at least 90cm between table edge and wall
- Between sofa and coffee table: 45–50cm
- Either side of a double bed: at least 60cm for getting in and out comfortably
- In front of a wardrobe or chest of drawers: at least 90cm to open doors or drawers

### Step 6: Save and Share

When you're happy with your layout, save or screenshot your plan. Use it when furniture shopping to double-check dimensions, show contractors what you're planning, or refer back to when you start moving things in real life.

## Room Planning Tips for Every Room Type

### Living Room

Anchor the seating around a focal point — this might be a fireplace, a TV, or a large window. Everything else radiates from there. Avoid pushing all your furniture against the walls; it creates a formal, cold feeling. Pull the sofa slightly away from the wall and it instantly feels more intimate.

Think about conversation. If two sofas are more than 3 metres apart, it becomes difficult to have a normal conversation without raising your voice. Aim for a sofa and armchair arrangement where the longest distance between seating is under 2.5–3m.

### Bedroom

In the bedroom, the bed is the non-negotiable anchor. Place it first — ideally against the wall opposite the door so it's the first thing you see when you enter. Then fit everything else around it. Don't sacrifice the clearance either side of the bed — those 60cm are worth protecting, even in a smaller room.

Natural light matters too. Where possible, avoid placing the bed directly under a window (draughts, light in your face in the morning) or directly opposite a window facing east (glare on your pillow at sunrise).

### Kitchen

Kitchen planning is more technical than other rooms because you're working around fixed infrastructure — plumbing, gas points, extractor positions. Focus first on the work triangle: the route between your hob, sink, and fridge should be efficient and unobstructed. The ideal triangle has sides between 1.2m and 2.7m.

Ensure at least 120cm of clearance between facing runs of units — this is the minimum for two people to work comfortably. Island units are fantastic for storage and prep space but need at least 100cm of clearance on every walkable side.

### Bathroom

Bathroom planning is about efficient use of a usually small space. The toilet needs at least 60cm of clearance in front of it and 15–20cm either side. The shower or bath needs to be accessible without squeezing past other fixtures. Doors — especially in small bathrooms — are often better replaced with sliding or pocket doors to save the swing clearance.

## Common Room Planning Mistakes to Avoid

### Buying Furniture Before You Plan

The most common and costly mistake. It seems obvious, but most people see a sofa they love and buy it before checking whether it actually fits the room. Always plan first, buy second.

### Ignoring the Door Swing

Every door needs clearance for its full arc. In a room planner, you can mark door swings visually. Make sure no furniture sits within that arc — a wardrobe door that constantly bashes into a bedside table is an enduring daily annoyance.

### Underestimating Traffic Flow

A room that looks balanced in a plan can feel cramped in real life if the walkways are too narrow. Be ruthless about maintaining the minimum 90cm walkway through any regularly used route.

### Planning in 2D Only

Top-down planning is excellent for layout, but remember that rooms have height too. A low ceiling with tall furniture can feel oppressive. Think about the vertical dimension separately from the floor plan.

### Forgetting About Radiators and Sockets

Placing a sofa against a radiator blocks heat. Placing a desk where there's no nearby socket means running extension cables. Mark radiators, sockets, and light switches on your plan — it takes two minutes and prevents a lot of frustration.
