## Why Bathroom Planning Is Different

Bathrooms present a unique planning challenge for a few reasons:

- They're often small — the average UK bathroom is around 4–5 square metres
- Every major element is fixed: toilet, basin, bath, and shower all involve plumbing
- Water and electricity are both present, creating strict safety zones that constrain where things can go
- Every element has a minimum clearance requirement for safe, comfortable use
- The door position is often fixed, and it frequently opens directly into the usable space

This combination of constraints means that bathroom planning is more about solving a puzzle within fixed parameters than freely designing a space. The good news is that once you know the parameters, the puzzle has clear solutions.

## Understanding Bathroom Clearances

Clearances are the minimum distances that need to be maintained around each bathroom fixture for it to be usable and meet UK building regulations. These aren't suggestions — they're the foundation of a workable bathroom plan.

### Toilet

- In front of toilet: at least 600mm (ideally 700–750mm)
- Each side of toilet: at least 200mm from the edge of the seat to any wall, and 150mm from the centre of the pan to any obstruction

### Basin

- In front of basin: at least 700mm
- Each side of basin: at least 200mm from the edge to any wall

### Bath

- Access side (long side): at least 700mm for getting in and out safely
- The other long side can abut the wall — this is the most common configuration

### Walk-in Shower Enclosure

- Minimum shower enclosure size: 800mm x 800mm (900mm x 900mm is much more comfortable)
- In front of shower door: at least 700mm clearance for opening and stepping in

Check these clearances on your plan before finalising anything. The most common planning mistakes are insufficient clearance in front of the toilet (the plan looks fine but real life is cramped) and shower doors that swing into the basin.

## The Most Common Bathroom Layouts

### Linear Layout (Fixtures on One Wall)

All fixtures run along one wall in a line: toilet, basin, then bath. This is the simplest layout and works well in long, narrow bathrooms. The advantage is that plumbing can be concentrated along a single wall. The limitation is that the bath is usually the furthest point from the door, which can feel awkward in small spaces.

### L-Shape Layout (Fixtures on Two Adjacent Walls)

The bath or shower goes on one wall; the toilet and basin go on the adjacent wall. This is one of the most flexible layouts because it works in a wide range of room shapes and creates natural separation between the wet zone (bath or shower) and the drier zone (toilet and basin).

### Parallel Layout (Fixtures on Opposite Walls)

The toilet and basin on one wall, bath or shower on the opposite wall. This only works with sufficient room width — you need at least 1.8m between the two walls to clear the bath access requirements on the bath side and leave walkway space. It's less common in UK homes but can work well in wider rooms.

### Wet Room Layout

A wet room replaces the shower enclosure with a fully waterproofed (tanked) floor with a central or wall drain. The whole room becomes the shower. Wet rooms work beautifully in square or nearly-square rooms and are increasingly popular in main bathrooms and en suites. They require specialist waterproofing and a properly designed drainage gradient, so always involve a professional.

## En Suite Bathroom Planning

En suites are typically smaller than main bathrooms, often carved out of a bedroom. They're usually shower rooms rather than full bathrooms (no bath). Key considerations for en suite planning:

- Minimum viable en suite size: approximately 1.5m x 2m (for a basic toilet + basin + compact shower)
- A door that swings inward is often impractical in a small en suite — consider a sliding or barn-style door
- The toilet should be visible from the doorway — bathrooms where you enter sideways towards the toilet feel disorienting
- Ventilation is essential: a window or extractor fan is required in all UK bathrooms
- Consider noise: en suites sharing a wall with a bedroom should use quiet-flush toilets and quality fittings

## Small Bathroom Planning Tips

Most UK bathrooms are small. Here's how to make every centimetre count.

### Go Wall-Hung

Wall-hung toilets and floating vanities create visual and physical floor space. They're easier to clean around, and they make a small bathroom feel larger because the eye can see the floor unbroken. The cistern is concealed in the wall — this takes a few centimetres from room depth, but the visual and practical gains are usually worth it.

### Choose the Right Shower Enclosure

In a small bathroom, the shower enclosure door choice matters a lot. A standard hinged door needs clearance in front — if you're tight on space, a sliding door or a quadrant enclosure in a corner takes up less floor space when in use.

### Use a Corner Basin

Corner basins are smaller than standard wall-mounted basins and they tuck into the corner without interrupting the floor plan. Great for compact en suites where every centimetre matters.

### Stack Your Storage

In small bathrooms, think vertically. Tall, slim storage cabinets or shelving above the toilet (ensuring at least 600mm clearance above the toilet cistern) can provide significant storage without consuming floor space.

### Plan Recessed Niches

A recessed niche in the shower wall or above the bath eliminates the need for a shelf or caddy and keeps the space looking clean and uncluttered. Plan these at the design stage — they need to go between studs or be built in before tiling.

### Consider a Sliding or Pocket Door

In a small bathroom, the door swing can consume a significant chunk of usable floor space. A sliding door or a pocket door (which slides into the wall cavity) eliminates the swing entirely and can free up surprisingly useful area. Plan this at the design stage, as pocket doors require the wall to be modified.

## Bathroom Planning FAQ

### Do I need planning permission to move a bathroom?

In most cases, no — moving a bathroom within the existing footprint of a house is considered permitted development. However, if you're changing the use of a room or adding a bathroom to a listed building, you may need consent. Always check with your local planning authority if in doubt.

### How much does it cost to move a toilet?

Moving a toilet a short distance (within the same room) typically costs £500–£1,500 depending on how much the waste pipe needs to be rerouted. Moving it to a completely different wall can cost significantly more. This is why planning with the soil pipe position in mind can save significant money.

### What's the minimum size for a bathroom with a bath?

The absolute minimum for a bathroom containing a standard bath (1700mm x 700mm), toilet, and basin is approximately 2.4m x 1.7m — but this will be extremely tight. A more comfortable minimum is 2.5m x 2m.

### Should I choose a shower over a bath?

If you have two bathrooms, it's generally worth keeping a bath in at least one for resale value. If you have only one bathroom, a bath with overhead shower or a separate shower enclosure alongside the bath gives you the most flexibility.

### What's the best type of shower tray for a small bathroom?

A low-profile or level-access shower tray (also called a walk-in tray) is the most practical option for small bathrooms. Combined with a sliding or bi-fold door, it minimises the floor space consumed by the shower zone.
