# Bedroom Floor Plan: How to Design the Perfect Bedroom Layout

A good bedroom floor plan creates a space that feels calm and restful, that functions well for dressing and storage, and that works for the way you actually live. Getting the layout right makes a real difference to your daily life — and it's much easier to get right with a plan than by trial and error with heavy furniture.

## Start With the Bed

The bed is the largest piece of furniture in any bedroom and the element everything else relates to. Place it first — and place it thoughtfully.

**The best positions for the bed:**

- **Centred on the wall opposite the door.** The classic and usually best position. You see the bed when you enter the room; it becomes the room's natural focal point. Equal space on both sides creates balance and makes the room feel welcoming.
- **Against a side wall.** Works in narrower rooms where centring the bed on the main wall would leave inadequate clearance on one or both sides. One side of the bed will be against the wall — ensure the person who sleeps on that side is comfortable with this before committing.
- **In an alcove.** A recessed alcove creates a natural sleeping zone with built-in visual separation from the rest of the room. Particularly good in larger bedrooms where a little definition between the sleeping area and a reading or dressing area is desirable.

**Positions to avoid:**

- Directly under a window — cold draughts in winter, light in the face at dawn, and difficulty with curtains or blinds
- Directly opposite a window facing east — sunrise glare on the pillow, which is unpleasant even with good curtains
- Where the door opens directly onto the foot of the bed — this feels abrupt and can cause safety issues if someone opens the door quickly

## Bedroom Clearances: The Numbers That Matter

The clearances around the bed determine whether a bedroom feels comfortable to live in or constantly slightly cramped:

- **Either side of the bed:** minimum 60cm, ideally 75cm. This is the space for getting in and out without squeezing.
- **At the foot of the bed:** minimum 90cm if there's a walkway or a wardrobe opposite
- **In front of a hinged wardrobe:** minimum 90cm for the doors to open fully without obstruction
- **In front of a chest of drawers:** minimum 90cm for all drawers to open fully

These clearances look small on a plan but feel significant in real life. A bedroom where you have to turn sideways to get between the bed and the wardrobe is one of those daily frustrations that makes a home feel like it doesn't quite work.

## Storage Planning in the Bedroom

Most bedrooms don't have enough storage, and the visual clutter that results — clothes on chairs, bags on the floor, surfaces piled with things — actively affects quality of sleep. Research consistently shows that cluttered bedrooms are associated with poorer sleep quality and higher stress.

### Small Bedroom Storage (Under 10 sq m)

- A floor-to-ceiling built-in wardrobe along one full wall or in the alcove is usually the most space-efficient solution — it uses the vertical space that freestanding wardrobes typically waste
- Under-bed storage with drawers or a lift-up ottoman base doubles the storage capacity of the room
- Wall-mounted bedside shelves instead of freestanding bedside tables free up visible floor space

### Medium Bedroom Storage (10–15 sq m)

- A freestanding wardrobe plus a chest of drawers handles most clothing and bedroom storage
- Bedside tables with drawers rather than open shelves — open shelves tend to accumulate clutter
- A window seat with storage below if there's a bay window

### Large Bedroom Storage (15+ sq m)

- A walk-in wardrobe or dressing room completely removes dressing and clothing storage from the sleeping space
- A dressing table in a window area — getting ready in natural light is a quality-of-life upgrade

## Designing the Bedroom for Two People

Two people sharing a bedroom means two daily routines that need to coexist without friction:

- **Equal access on both sides of the bed** — minimum 60cm each side, regardless of room size
- **Two bedside tables** — both people need a surface for a phone, lamp, and glass of water
- **Accessible storage for both** — each person should have a clearly defined allocation of wardrobe and drawer space
- **Getting-ready zone** — two people trying to use the same mirror or the same socket in the same corner every morning creates daily friction. Plan for two people at the design stage.
