## What Is a Floor Plan Template?

A floor plan template is a pre-built room outline — typically a rectangular or L-shaped room with doors and windows already positioned — that you adapt to match your actual space. Templates save you the time of building the room outline from scratch and can help you visualise what a standard arrangement might look like before you apply it to your specific room.

Templates are most useful when:

- You're planning a common room type in a standard size (a rectangular bedroom, a galley kitchen, an L-shaped living room)
- You want to see what a typical arrangement of that room type looks like before trying your own layout
- Your room is a standard shape and you don't need to spend time building the outline from scratch

## Types of Floor Plan Templates

### Bedroom Templates

Standard bedroom templates are typically rectangular with a door on one wall and a window on the opposite or adjacent wall — the most common configuration in UK houses. A single bedroom template (roughly 3m x 3.5m) and a double bedroom template (roughly 3.5m x 4m) cover the majority of standard UK bedroom types. Master bedroom templates are usually 4m x 4m or larger with an en suite attached.

### Living Room Templates

Living room templates for UK homes typically include a chimney breast or fireplace on one wall, a bay window, and two doors — reflecting the common layout of Victorian and Edwardian terraced houses. Modern flat living room templates often show a more open-plan arrangement with the kitchen at one end. Long, narrow living room templates reflect the specific challenge of converted terraced houses.

### Kitchen Templates

Kitchen templates come in the standard layout configurations: galley (two parallel runs of units), L-shaped (units on two adjacent walls), U-shaped (units on three walls), and with island (any of the above with a central island). Each template shows the basic unit positions, appliance spaces, and door and window positions for that layout type.

### Bathroom Templates

Small bathroom templates show the standard UK bathroom (bath, toilet, and basin in a 2.5m x 2m space). En suite templates show compact shower room configurations in spaces from 1.5m x 2m. Wet room templates show open-plan shower configurations. These bathroom templates are particularly useful because bathroom layouts are so constrained by clearance requirements and plumbing positions.

## How to Use a Template Effectively

A template is a starting point, not a finished plan. Here's how to use one well:

1. **Start with the template that most closely matches your room shape** — rectangular, L-shaped, or with specific features like a bay window or chimney breast
2. **Adjust the dimensions to match your actual room measurements.** This is the most important step. A template with the wrong dimensions will give you misleading results about what actually fits.
3. **Move the doors and windows to match your real room's layout.** Door positions especially can vary significantly from the template default. The position and swing direction of the door is critical to getting a layout that works.
4. **Use the template's furniture arrangement as inspiration, not instruction.** Try the standard arrangement first to understand why it's common, then experiment with alternatives.
5. **Add your specific furniture dimensions.** A template uses generic sizes; your actual furniture may be meaningfully different. A 3-seater sofa can range from 180cm to 240cm wide depending on the model.

## When a Template Isn't Right

Templates work well for standard rectangular rooms. They're less useful for:

- Rooms with unusual shapes — alcoves, chimney breasts that project significantly into the room, bay windows that affect the usable layout, L-shaped rooms
- Very small rooms where every centimetre matters and the template's standard dimensions don't reflect your specific space
- Commercial spaces like restaurants or offices, which have more complex planning requirements than standard room templates accommodate

For these situations, building your room from scratch in a planner — which takes only a few minutes — gives you a more accurate result than adapting a template that doesn't quite match your space.
