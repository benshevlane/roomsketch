# Studio Apartment Floor Plans: Making One Room Do Everything

A studio apartment asks one room to do an extraordinary amount of work. It needs to serve as your bedroom, living room, and often your home office, all without feeling like any of those things individually. Getting the floor plan right is the key to making a studio feel like a proper home rather than a large hotel room.

## The Core Challenge of a Studio Floor Plan

In a regular flat, different rooms provide physical and psychological separation between activities: sleep in the bedroom, work in the office, relax in the living room. In a studio, those separations need to be created artificially within a single space.

The floor plan is your primary tool for doing this. A good studio floor plan creates distinct zones for sleeping, working, and living — zones that feel separate even though they share the same four walls. A poor studio floor plan has everything jumbled together in a way that feels restless, undefined, and difficult to relax in.

---

## The Sleeping Zone: Your Non-Negotiable

The bed is the dominant element in any studio. It's the largest piece of furniture and it sets the tone for the whole space. The key question is where to put it.

**Far wall from the entrance.** Placing the bed at the far end from the front door creates a natural graduation from public to private as you move through the space. The living area is closest to the door; the sleeping area is furthest. This is the most psychologically comfortable arrangement for most people and is usually the best starting point.

**In an alcove.** If your studio has an alcove or recessed section, this is a natural sleeping zone. The physical definition of the alcove helps separate the sleeping area from the rest of the studio — you're stepping into a specific, defined space when you go to bed, which makes the psychological separation easier.

**Screened off.** A room divider, a bookshelf, or a tall upholstered headboard can create visual separation between the bed and the living area without using any additional floor space. This works particularly well in studios where the bed must occupy a central position.

**Murphy bed or quality sofa bed.** In very small studios (under 30 square metres), a Murphy bed that folds up against the wall during the day, or a high-quality sofa bed, allows the sleeping zone to transform into the living zone. The quality point matters: a cheap sofa bed that's uncomfortable to sleep on undermines both the sofa and the sleeping functions simultaneously.

---

## The Living Zone: Creating Distance From the Bed

The living zone should feel as far from the bed as possible, even if the actual physical distance is only a few metres. A sofa facing away from the bed, with its back acting as a visual barrier, helps create this distance. A rug under the sofa and coffee table further reinforces the zone — the rug defines where the living area begins and ends.

If you have a window, orient the living zone towards it. Natural light and a visual connection to the outside world make the living zone feel more spacious and pleasant for spending time in. A bed facing a window and a sofa facing away from a window is usually the wrong arrangement in a small studio.

---

## The Work Zone: Psychological Separation Matters

Working from home in a studio is genuinely challenging, because your workspace and your relaxation space are the same room. The biggest risk is that work bleeds into every part of the day because it's always visible.

To make it work:

- Put the desk in a dedicated position — ideally not directly visible from the bed or from the sofa
- Keep the desk tidy at the end of every workday. A clear desk boundary signals the psychological end of work to your brain. An untidy desk that's always in sight means work never really ends.
- A fold-down desk mounted on the wall provides the most complete separation — when it's folded up, the work zone simply ceases to exist visually
- Some studio dwellers use a room divider behind the desk to create a more defined work boundary between the desk and the relaxation zone

## Studio Floor Plans by Size

### Small Studio (25–35 sq m)

At this size, you're making hard choices. The essentials are a bed, a sofa, and a desk — everything else needs to justify its floor space. Consider a lofted sleeping area if ceiling height allows (this is particularly common in warehouse conversions). Wall-mounted and fold-away furniture is especially valuable at this size.

### Medium Studio (35–50 sq m)

More room to breathe, and the possibility of more distinct zones. A partial room divider (a bookshelf, a slatted screen, or a low partition) between the sleeping and living zones is feasible. A small two-seater sofa plus an armchair creates a proper living arrangement. A small dining table might be possible — or a bar-height counter that doubles as a dining surface and workspace.

### Large Studio (50+ sq m)

A large studio has the floor space of a proper one-bedroom flat — the only difference is the lack of a separate bedroom wall. At this size, you can plan genuinely distinct zones for living, sleeping, dining, and working. Use furniture arrangement and rugs rather than walls to define each zone; the visual separation can feel as complete as physical walls if done well.
